title = "Metallicity"
+++

Source: [TW](https://x.com/blog_supplement/status/2050450317896991049)

The general theory to date has been that the universe needs time before it can start forming planets. The earliest stars have low metallicity, i.e., elements higher than H and He. As per the current models, only when these higher elements emerge can they form dust and organic compounds, and the dust can coalesce to form planets and life. 

In the early galaxies, the paucity of carbon and the intense, unshielded UV radiation from young stars were believed to prevent the formation of complex molecules. 

However, JWST has obtained one of its most remarkable results to date: The dwarf galaxy Sextans A with a redshift  z > 10, and just 7% of the Sun’s metallicity is already producing dust in dense pockets only a few light-years across. 

These islands produce fresh polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, rather than just generating them from dying stars. These act as "protected regions" where gas density is high enough to shield the organic compounds from destructive UV radiation. These PAHs are mixed in with metallic iron and silicon carbide formed by aging stars in Sextans A to generate "organo-metallic" dust grains. This suggests that the universe is geared to produce dust, planets and likely life even in low metallicity environments, contrary to what was previously predicted.  

Thus, the universe was probably "planet-ready" and "organic chemistry-ready" much earlier than was thought.
