The grave of Joseph von Fraunhofer, who came from a long line of German glassmakers and died at the young age of 39, reads: "Aproximavit sidera" = "He brought closer the stars." 

## Lenses
From my inquiries, it seems this epitaph was originally meant to indicate the fact that he made some of the finest telescopes of the age without chromatic aberration  
and figured out various formulations for making the highest quality optical glass and objective lenses.  

It is believed that most of his secrets of glass- and lens-making died with him as he was poisoned by the heavy metals used in the process. 

## Spectroscope
However, he is best remembered for inventing the spectroscope in 1814 CE and for discovering spectral lines. He followed up by making extremely fine silver wire and a grating from it that could diffract light. Thus, he devised the first effective diffraction grating for generating spectra. 

In the process, he performed the engineering feat of obtaining stellar spectra and found that the spectral lines of the sun differed from the lines found in the spectra of several bright stars: Sirius, Castor, Pollux, Capella, and Betelgeuse. 

He found that while Capella's lines were similar to the sun (they are the same spectral class), the other differed quite a bit. 

Thus, he reasoned that they were not entirely caused by the earth's atmosphere but were intrinsic to the nature of the stars.  While he did not realize the connection to chemistry (that was to be discovered by Kirchhoff and Bunsen in 1859 CE), he laid the foundation for understanding the composition of the stars. 

He had thereby provided the first physical proof that the stars are like the sun and not like the flame of the fire in which he found yellow emission lines. 

Thus, he literally "brought closer the stars!" though that was not the reason why the statement was put on his grave.
