Can archaeology tell the whole story? This is a classic example of how archaeology with no philological or epigraphic material leaves us with a cliffhanger with no ending:
https://nature.com/articles/s41562-025-02399-9
The authors report a detailed archaeo-genetic study of an unusual massacre site in northern Serbia in the Carpathian basin from the 800s BCE. It is one of two known close-by mass graves, exclusively of tens of women and children. They were killed by horseback riders using axes, spears and arrows as they were running away or retreating. They were subsequently buried in a mass grave with some ritual accompaniment with offerings of pottery, metalwork, grain and meat.

What exactly happened here? That is what archaeology does not entirely answer. The exclusive massacre of women and children is unusual: as the authors agree, they are usually enslaved. So was this an ethnic cleansing followed by their burial by their own people with ritual offerings? Or was it a mass sacrifice of human booty by the attackers?  Then there is the question of who did this and who were the victims. The genetics suggests that the victims were likely IE- speaking local people from Carpathians. We know around this time there were incursions from the steppes called the Thraco-Cimmerian movements. Or was it an early westward thrust of the shaka-s (Scythians)? There are Thraco-Cimmerian items in this massacre site. Could it be that steppe Cimmerians or shaka-s carried out a mass massacre that was latter seen among hUNa-s or Mongols? That remains a possibility but an open question.
