Sujanagar Stone Inscription of the Time of Bhojavarman, Year 7

RYOSUKE FURUI
Institute for Advanced Studies on Asia, The University of Tokyo

Abstract: The present article offers the edition of the Sujanagar stone inscription of the time of Bhojavarman, year 7, with notes and translation, and the discussion on its contents and implications. The inscription records the two acts of mahāsāmanta Avūdeva, a subordinate ruler of Bhojavarman, in relation to a religious institution called a vihāra protected by Allahabhaṭṭārakasvāmin, namely, the withdrawal of cash endowment and the donation of right to tax at a market. It is the sole inscription mentioning the activity of a subordinate ruler under the Varmans. The transactions recorded in it attests to the developed monetary economy based on cowrie-shells. The inscription can also be the earliest epigraphic evidence for the presence of Islam and Muslims in Bengal, with possible references to a subordinate ruler from Arab or Persian merchant family and his establishment of a madrasa, though we need more evidence to buttress this possibility.

Keywords: Bengal, inscription, twelfth century, the Varmans, subordinate ruler, monetary economy, Islam.

Introduction

The inscription to be dealt with in the present article is the one currently kept at Bangladesh National Museum with Accession Number E-67.232. It is an inscription engraved on a thick slab of black stone, 43 cm long and 31 cm wide, of which a portion was broken off at the lower right corner. The inscription was discovered by an accidental digging at village Sujanagar in Sadar upazila of Munshiganj district, Bangladesh, and acquired by the museum in 1967 (Biswas 2007; Islam 2010: 113). The stone is inscribed lengthways in nine lines and has an image of a four-legged animal facing left engraved at the lower left corner (Plate 1). The script used in the inscription is Gauḍī datable to the twelfth century, and this dating is supported by the reference to mahārājādhirāja Bhojavarman as a reigning king. The engraving is rather shallow and partly disturbed by cracks and scratches on the surface of the slab. Despite that, the akṣaras are mostly readable except the two in lines 3 and 4, of which the shapes pose some difficulty for identification. The language is Sanskrit, mostly correct and comprehensible but showing some orthographical and grammatical irregularities and mistakes in some parts, as mentioned in the notes to the text.

The inscription was first notified by Swapan Kumar Biswas (Biswas 2007). Then late Shariful Islam attempted at its decipherment and edition (Islam 2010). However, he could not read the inscription properly and his discussion went astray due to the undue attachment to the image of animal, which he wrongly identified as a dog despite that he correctly transcribed a reference to ass (gardabha) in the inscription (Islam 2010: 117).1 As a result, the inscription is yet to be properly deciphered and its contents, which have critical bearings on the early medieval history of Bengal, have not been published. In this article, I will provide an edition of the inscription with textual notes and translation, and discuss the contents of the inscription and their implications for the history of early medieval Bengal.

Translation

Success! In the year seven of his majesty mahārājādhirāja illustrious Bhojavarmadeva, on the day 21 of month Jyaiṣṭha by the movement of the Sun, during the administration of the appointed official (adhikṛta) Jīvasena (ll. 1–2).3
With the assent of all the kinsmen of the other countries, by mahāsāmanta illustrious Avūdeva, the son of pañcakulika Hāsi, for the protection of sureties, the donation of unbroken circulating cowrie-shells, which was incurred of loss by conch-shell workers, was broken, and for repairs of the vihāra protected by Allahabhaṭṭārakasvāmin, fixed tax on seizing at market, which was (Avūdeva’s) own enjoyment (bhoga) as mahāsāmanta, was given (ll. 2–7). For those who protect this pious deed, there would be this religious merit. On the side of them, the mother+++(=मतेत्य् उक्तम्, न मातेति!!)+++ will be the one making pure salutation (ll. 7–8). The father of those who do otherwise again will be an ass (ll. 8–9).

Discussions

Contents of the Inscription

The inscription records the two acts by mahāsāmanta Avūdeva in connection with a religious institution. The time of the acts is said to be in the seventh year of the reign of king Bhojavarman (l. 1). The title of mahārājādhirāja and the find-spot locatable in the sub-region of Vaṅga points to his identification with the Varman king of the same name known from the Belava Copperplate Inscription, dated year 5 of his reign (Majumdar 1929: 14–24). It places the date of the inscription to the middle of the twelfth century.

What is notable about the date in this inscription is the expression ‘by the movement of the sun’ (sūryagatyā) preceding the month and day, Jyaiṣṭha 21 (l. 2). It clearly indicates the use of solar dates, which is presumed by the number of days larger than 15 and the absence of the reference to pakṣa (fortnight).4 The same expression is also used in a manuscript of the Vimalaprabhā mentioning the 39th year of the reign of mahārājādhirāja Harivarman, the Mainamati grant of Raṇavaṅkamalla Harikāladeva mentioning the reign of Harikāladeva and the Sobharampur plate of Dāmodaradeva (Sanderson 2009: 82, note 136; Bhattacharyya 1933: 287, l. 24; Dani 1953–4: 188, l. 35).5 The prevalence of solar dates in early Bengal could be conjectured by the absence of pakṣa and the occurrence of days larger than 15 in most of the inscriptions, with a few exceptions clearly referring to the lunar dates.6 The four cases including the present inscription provide clear evidence for its use in the eastern part of Bengal in the period between the second half of the eleventh century and the early thirteenth century.

The time of the acts is also stated in reference to the administration of Jīvasena, the appointed official (adhikṛta) (l. 2). The fact that the inscription presumably commissioned by mahāsāmanta Avūdeva had to mention not only the reign of Bhojavarman but also the administration of Jīvasena shows the position of the latter superior to Avūdeva and the presence of an appointed official between the king and his subordinate ruler.

As is evident from his title, mahāsāmanta Avūdeva, the protagonist of the inscription, was a subordinate ruler of Bhojavarman (l. 4). His father Hāsi was pañcakulika (l. 3), a leader or a member of pañcakula which denoted an urban administrative organization in charge of documentary works and exchange centre called maṇḍapikā, mainly consisting of urban influential people, according to the inscriptional evidences of the early medieval western India (Sircar 1966: 230; Mita 2017: 62–72). It indicates the urban or rather mercantile background of Avūdeva, whose kinsmen were located abroad and possibly engaging in commercial activities, as will be discussed below.

The two acts of Avūdeva recorded in the inscription are breaking ‘the donation of unbroken circulating cowrie-shells’ for the

[[118]]

protection of sureties and donating ‘fixed tax on seizing at market’ for the repairs of a vihāra (ll. 4–7). The appearance of both the acts in the same inscription suggests their interconnection.

As for the first act (ll. 4–5), ‘donation of unbroken circulating cowries’ (akhaṇḍitāvārttika-kapardakadāna) can be interpreted as monetary endowment of which interest generated by the investment is used for particular purposes without reducing the original capital, in line with the arrangement called akṣayanīvī.7 In the present case, endowment was made in cowrie-shells which would circulate as currency and generate interest without reducing the original amount. Breaking of this donation seems to mean liquidation or withdrawal of the endowed cash.+++(4)+++ The assent of all the kinsmen required for this act suggests that they collectively raised the fund with Avūdeva for the endowment. Their location abroad, expressed by the word ‘of the other countries’ (pāradeśika) (ll. 2–3), and the supposed monetary contribution point to their commercial activities based on the kinship relation, which may have formed a trade network. Avūdeva could communicate with them through this network to gain their assent.

The cause of the liquidation of endowment can be conjectured from the word śaṃkhakara[pra]prahāra, which is emended to śaṃkhakāraprāptahāra, ‘incurred of loss by conch-shell workers’, and the purpose stated as ‘for the protection of sureties’ (l. 4). The sequence of events can be reconstructed as follows: The fund of cowrie-shells was given to the vihāra protected by Allahabhaṭṭārakasvāmin, the religious institution for which the second act of Avūdeva was made. The fund had to be invested for interest, and conch-shell workers were deposited of it with appointment of sureties. However, the conch-shell workers incurred loss on the original capital, for which the sureties were required to compensate. To exempt the latter from this liability and cover the loss, Avūdeva once liquidated the endowment and made an alternative arrangement for the religious institution.

The second act, namely, the donation for the repairs of the vihāra, was the alternative arrangement necessitated by the liquidation (ll. 5–7). The object of donation is called ‘fixed tax on seizing at market’ (haṭṭagrahakḷpta), which seems to denote tax on seized or confiscated commodities charged at a market.8 Notably, it is said to be his own enjoyment (bhoga) as mahāsāmanta, meaning that the right to charge this tax was held by Avūdeva as a privilege accompanying his position. The donation in this case denotes the transference of this right to the religious institution in lieu of the withdrawn monetary fund.

In view of the purpose of donation, the donee was presumed to be the vihāra protected by the deity called Allahabhaṭṭārakasvāmin (ll. 5–6). The name of the deity and the choice of vihāra as a term denoting an institution associated with him, not devakula, āyatana or maṭha which are generally applied to an abode of Brahmanical deities, indicates their special character. The former may be Allah, the Islamic God, and the latter a madrasa, the theological school for which the term denoting a centre for learning and other activities of Buddhists was chosen due to similarity in functions.

The inscription ends with the two benedictory and imprecatory sentences (ll. 7–9), of which the second has a corresponding image engraved on the stone. Accordingly, the image of a four-legged animal must represent an ass, which the father of a transgressor is destined to be reborn. The other point notable about this sentence is the word punar, ‘again’. The withdrawal of endowment by Avūdeva was deemed the first breach which should not be repeated, so that the word ‘again’ was added.

The last to say about the contents of the inscription is the absence of toponyms. There is no reference to place names of either the administrative unit, territory of Avūdeva or localities where the concerned market or the vihāra were. This absence may point to the fixation of the inscribed slab to the vihāra itself, which made the indication of its location unnecessary. Both the vihāra and market may have existed in proximity within the area controlled by Avūdeva, so that the locations of all the three were obvious to the intended readers of the inscription.

Implications

The contents of the inscription have important implications for the early medieval history of Bengal, especially for the administration under the Varmans, the economic condition of the period and the possible presence of Islam and Muslims.

The present inscription is so far the sole record which gives a glimpse of the activity of subordinate rulers under the Varmans. In view of the title pañcakulika wielded by his father and the spread of his relatives in the other countries, Avūdeva seems to have originated from a mercantile family and acquired the position of mahāsāmanta through the service to the Varman king. This case can be compared with that of the lineage of mahāsāmanta Bhadrapāṇa serving the Pāla king Dharmapāla: his great-grandfather was a caravan trader (sārthavāha) and father a mahāsāmanta (Furui 2011: 153, ll. 47–9).

What should be noted in the present case is the presence of adhikṛta Jīvasena between the king and his subordinate ruler. Though the content or unit of his administration (vyāpāra) is not clearly stated, the reference to it just after the reign of the king in the inscription suggests the jurisdiction of Jīvasena covering the territory of Avūdeva. The formation of such an administration above subordinate rulers and the appointment of an official in charge of it points to the attempt of the Varman kings to keep control over their subordinate rulers.

The acts recorded in the inscription presupposed the developed condition of monetary economy. Monetary deposit in cowrie-shells was viable only with their circulation in abundance as currency. The assumed contribution to the fund by Avūdeva’s kinsmen living abroad attests to the sphere of circulation of this currency beyond Bengal or the channels which could convert their wealth accumulated in different forms into it through exchange. The expected generation of profit through the management of fund by conch-shell workers, though failed in the present case, was conditioned by the existence of vibrant market economy in which their products circulated as commodity and could be sold for cash.

Such an economic condition can also be deduced from the second act, in which the right to tax from a market was transferred to a religious institution. Its transference as a substitute for withdrawn monetary endowment, or its assignment as a privilege of a subordinate ruler before that, was feasible only with vigorous commercial activities around the market. The trade network connecting the market with the other countries, which was conjectured from the activities of Avūdeva’s kinsmen, may have contributed to the thriving market economy.

Finally, the donor and donee of the present inscription can imply the presence of Islam and Muslims in the early medieval Bengal. The name of deity Allahabhaṭṭārakasvāmin could be composed of the transliteration of Allah suffixed with two titles denoting ‘master’, both typical of Brahmanical deities. With choice of vihāra as discussed above, he could be identified with the Islamic God. Accordingly, the donor could be a Muslim and the names of him and his father, Avūdeva and Hāsi, the transliterations of Arabic or Persian ones. The presence of their kinsmen in the other countries, on the other hand, suggests their foreign origin. Hāsi may have come to and settled in Bengal as an Arab or Persian merchant engaging in trade activities.

The presence of Arab Muslims on the coastal area of western India in the early medieval period is well-attested by the inscriptional evidence. The Chinchani copperplate inscription of the time of the Rāṣṭrakūṭa king Indra III, dated year 848 Śaka Era (AD 926), mentions Madhumati, the son of Sahiyārahāra, of Tājika lineage (anvaya) as a ruler (nṛpati) who was given Saṃyānamaṇḍala from Kṛṣṇarāja and conquered all the chiefs of harbours (Sircar 1957–8: 52, ll. 19–22, vv. 17–19). He and his father were clearly Arabs with the Arabic and Persian names Muhammad and Shahriyar, which were transliterated in Sanskrit as such.9 Similarly, a lineage of Arab ship-owners serving the Kadamba kings of Goa is known from the Panjim plates of Jayakeśin I, year 981 Śaka Era (AD 1059). There was ship-owner (nauvittaka)10 Āliyama born to Tājīya lineage (vaṃśa) originating from Vaimūlyapura. His son

[[120]]

was Madhumada, whose son Sādhana served Keśirāja (Jayakeśin I) (Moraes 1931: 396, ll. 4–7). According to the other inscriptions, Madhumada or Madumod was a rich merchant of Goa who saved king Guhalladeva II with his wealth, when the ship of the latter stranded on the way to Somanātha for pilgrimage (Moraes 1931: 171–2). Sādhana or Sadano, here mentioned as his grandson, served Guhalladeva II and Jayakeśin I and was appointed a governor of Konkan by the latter (Moraes 1931: 185).

The Veraval inscriptions of year AH 662/1320 Vikrama Era/945 Valabhī (Gupta) Era/151 Siṃha Era (AD 1264), on the other hand, attests to the establishment of Islamic institution by a Muslim merchant (Sircar 1960–1). It records the establishment of a masjid (mijigitidharmasthāna) at Somanāthadevapattana by nakhudā Noradina Pīroja (Nuruddin Firuz), the son of nauvittaka Avūvrāhima (Abu Ibrahim) from Hurmuja (Hormuz) (Sircar 1960–1: 147, ll. 10–11, 148, ll. 19–20).11

The case of Avūdeva in the present inscription shows some similarities to these cases. Like Madhumati under the Rāṣṭrakūṭas and Sādhana under the Kadambas of Goa, he could be an Arab Muslim with mercantile background who gained political power by serving a local dynasty and acquiring the position of a subordinate ruler. His establishment of a vihāra, or rather madrasa, of Allahabhaṭṭārakasvāmin was comparable with the construction of a mijigiti by Noradina Pīroja. The inclusion of the Bay of Bengal within the orbit of the Arab trade activities and the existence of ports like Samandar frequented by Arab merchants in the early medieval period also support this plausibility, even though the Bengal coast did not assume a premier position like the western coasts in the Indian Ocean trade (Chakravarti 2002a). If proved as such, the present inscription is the earliest to mention the activity of Muslims in Bengal.

The evidence is, however, yet decisive. Unlike the Chinchani and Panjim plates mentioning Tājika and Tājīya as lineages of Madhumati and Sādhana, the present inscription has no comparable reference to the ethnic affiliation of Avūdeva and Hāsi. The correspondence of their names with the Arabic or Persian counterparts needs to be established by the specialists of these languages. The location of their kinsmen is just vaguely stated as the other countries without any specification. Thus the evidence provided by the inscription is not enough to reach a conclusion beyond plausibility.

Concluding Remarks

In spite of its brevity, the Sujanagar inscription has rich contents from which we can draw much information on the political, economic, and even religious structures and conditions of eastern Bengal in the middle of the twelfth century, as discussed above. The conclusions and possibilities offered in the present study, however, need to be buttressed by more evidences, which are especially required for the Islamic presence in early medieval Bengal suggested at the end of the previous section. Though the finding of the inscription by chance makes the possibility quite slim, the discovery of the structures of the concerned vihāra or the corresponding inscription in Arabic or Persian, like the case of the Veraval inscription (Sircar 1960–1: 149–50), through the excavation or exploration would change the situation drastically. I hope that further researches in the area around Sujanagar bring out new evidences which would enrich our discussion, no matter buttressing or rejecting the possibility raised by the present study.
