Mahmud of Ghazni‚Äôs Indian soldiers

Mahmud of Ghazni was the son of one Sabuktigin, who was a Turk slave-soldier working for the Persian princedoms of this diverse, violent region. Between violent kings and peaceful ones

In 1024, Mahmud of Ghazni led a campaign in Bundelkhand, ostensibly to punish the Chandella dynasty of Khajuraho for attacking the king of Kannauj, his vassal. So where does this leave us as far as the ‚ÄúIslamic invasions‚Äù of India are concerned? The ‚ÄòHindu vs Muslim‚Äô binary is as simplistic as the ‚Äòbad conquering Turk vs good syncretic Sufi‚Äô binary that our politics have been reduced to. A violent king is just as capable of acts of cultural accommodation as a ‚Äòpeaceful‚Äô one, and neither is a model to follow in a modern democratic nation-state.

At the end of the day, the arrival of Turkic warlords and troops in Northwest India was a multicultural and multi-ethnic process, involving flows of peoples and ideas in both directions between two cultural zones. These zones were more distinct and the exchanges larger in scale than earlier centuries, but they were not fundamentally unique in character. Interactions were more about acts of elite accommodation and friction than a major transformation in the material lives of most of our ancestors. The stories we make up about medieval kings say much more about us than they do about them.
