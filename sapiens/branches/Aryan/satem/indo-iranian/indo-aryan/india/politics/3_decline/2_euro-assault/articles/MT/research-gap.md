## Bird cataloguer
> there was a British army officer sent to fight in the Revolt of 1857 in north India who somehow found the time to observe and make notes on the 200+ bird species he saw in Awadh and Kumaon. He published these in 1861.

As we have said many times before, this was the thing that ultimately made the difference between us and the English. It was not so much the specifics like telegraph or sikhs or the shinde. There were several such English officers. 

One who immediately comes to mind is Sleeman, who discovered the first remains of a Titanosaurian sauropod at Jabalpur which led to the prolific English paleontologist Lydekker describing some of the first Indian Mesozoic dinosaurs.

It also brings to mind the great German WW1 soldier and entomologist Ernst Jünger.

## Lydekker
Source: [TW](https://x.com/blog_supplement/status/2051537160331792678)

Richard Lydekker, scholar of geology, paleontology and comparative anatomy at the Geological Survey of India from 1874 to 1881. He described numerous fossil amphibians, reptiles, and mammals from different Indian formations, including: 

1. First Indian fossil gharial; 
2. First detailed description of the ancient temnospondyl amphibians of India
3. 1st account of Indian rhynchosaurs
4. The fossil giraffe Sivatherium
5. The gigantic predator Hyainailouros, one of the last of the hyaenodont mammals.
6. The fossil orangutan Sivapithecus
7. The primitive elephant Stegoloxodon
8. The fossil horse Hipparion
