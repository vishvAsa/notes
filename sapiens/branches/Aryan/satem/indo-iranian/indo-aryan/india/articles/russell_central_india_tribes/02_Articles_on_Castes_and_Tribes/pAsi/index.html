01. The nature and origin of the caste

02. BrƒÅhmanical legends

The following tradition is related by the PƒÅsis themselves in Mƒ´rzƒÅpur and the Central Provinces: One day a man was going to kill a number of cows. Whatever their origin may have been the composition of the caste is now of a very mixed nature. Several names of other castes, as G≈´jar, GuƒÅl or Ahƒ´r, Arakh, Khatƒ´k, Bahelia, Bhƒ´l and Bania, are returned as divisions of the PƒÅsis in the United Provinces. The principal subcastes in the Central Provinces are the RƒÅjpƒÅsis or highest class, who probably were at one time landowners; the KaithwƒÅs or KaithmƒÅs, supposed to be descended from a KƒÅyasth, as already related; the Tirsulia, who take their name from the tris≈´la or three-bladed knife used to pierce the stem of the palm tree; the Bahelia or hunters, and ChiriyamƒÅr or fowlers; the Ghudchadha or those who ride on ponies, these being probably saises or horse-keepers; the Khatƒ´k or butchers and G≈´jar or graziers; and the MƒÅngta or beggars, these being the bards and genealogists of the caste, who beg from their clients and take food from their hands; they are looked down on by the other PƒÅsis.

04. Marriage and other customs

In the Central Provinces the tribe have now no exogamous groups; they avoid marriage with blood relations as far back as their memory carries them. At their weddings the couple walk round the srƒÅwan or heavy log of wood, which is dragged over the fields before sowing to break up the larger clods of earth. In the absence of this an ordinary plough or harrow will serve as a substitute, though why the PƒÅsis should impart a distinctively agricultural implement into their marriage ceremony is not clear. Like the Gonds, the PƒÅsis celebrate their weddings at the bridegroom‚Äôs house and not at the bride‚Äôs. Before the wedding the bridegroom‚Äôs mother goes and sits over a well, taking with her seven urad cakes5 and stalks of the plant. The bridegroom walks seven times round the well, and at each turn the parapet is marked with red and white clay and his mother throws one of the cakes and stalks into the well. Finally, the mother threatens to throw herself into the well, and the bridegroom begs her not to do so, promising that he will serve and support her. Divorce and the remarriage of widows are freely permitted. Conjugal morality is somewhat lax, and Mr. Crooke quotes a report from PertƒÅbgarh to the effect that if a woman of a tribe become pregnant by a stranger and the child be born in the house of her father or husband, it will be accepted as a PƒÅsi of pure blood and admitted to all tribal privileges. The bodies of adults may be buried or burnt as convenient, but those of children or of persons dying from smallpox, cholera or snake-bite are always buried. Mourning is observed during ten days for a man and nine days for a woman, while children who die unmarried are not mourned at all.

05. Religion, superstitions and social customs

The PƒÅsis worship all the ordinary Hindu deities. All classes of BrƒÅhmans will officiate at their marriages and other ceremonies, and do anything for them which does not involve touching them or any article in their houses. In Bengal, Sir H. At their marriage festivals, Mr. Gayer notes, boys are dressed up as girls and made to dance in public, but they do not use drums or other musical instruments. Marriage questions are decided by the tribal council, which is presided over by a chairman (Chaudhri) selected at each meeting from among the most influential adult males present. 06. Occupation

The traditional occupation of the PƒÅsis, as already stated, is the extraction of the sap of palm trees. But some of them are hunters and fowlers like the PƒÅrdhis, and like them also they make and mend grindstones, while others are agriculturists; and the caste has also strong criminal propensities, and includes a number of professional thieves. Some are employed in the NƒÅgpur mills and others have taken small building contracts. PƒÅsis are generally illiterate and in poor circumstances, and are much addicted to drink. In climbing6 palm trees to tap them for their juice the worker uses a heel-rope, by which his feet are tied closely together. At the same time he has a stout rope passing round the tree and his body. He leans back against this rope and presses the soles of his feet, thus tied together, against the tree. He then climbs up the tree by a series of hitches or jerks of his back and feet alternately.
