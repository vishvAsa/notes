This is a 17th-century English letter (old spelling) from Henry Revington to Shivaji Maharaj. The highlighted parts in the image match the transcription. 

This is a polite but firm diplomatic complaint from an English factor (merchant/official) to Shivaji Maharaj about mistreatment by his forces during conflicts around Rajapur.
</details>



**(4)**

**F. R. Rajapur** } **HENRY REVINGTON TO SIVAJI** **(Dated Rajapore**  
**p. 109.** } **13 Feb. 1659/60.**

**To Sevagy, Generall of the Hendoo Forces.**

How much freindshipp the English hath promised to act for  
you against Danda Rajapore Castle your servants Dorogy etc.  
wee beleive hath informed you; but how much injury wee have  
received from them wee are ashamed to tell you. 

Only thus much  
bee pleased to understand: that because wee would not take the  
jounkes lying in Rajapore River, and bee enemies to those who  
were our freinds, therefore hath our broker and one Englishman  
binn carryed away by your servants and kept and abused in  
prison for 25 dayes; and although now our broker is released, yet  
still the English man is detayned and imprisoned in Carrapatann  
Castle, to the sorrow of us and feare of all merchants in the port  
townes under your command, beleiving that this action will  
hinder there and our trading. But wee are patient and hope for  
satisfaction by a letter from you unto the cheife of theis your  
forces. Therefore wee pray that theis may come to your hands,  
and intreat your answer to this for the restoring of our English  
man and what else hath binn taken, against your command.

**[E. F. India, 1655-1660. pp. 358-9.]**


## Modern English Translation

**(Page 4)**

**F. R. Rajapur, p. 109**  
**HENRY REVINGTON TO SIVAJI**  
**(Dated Rajapore, 13 February 1659/60)**

**To shivAjI, General of the Hindu Forces.**

We believe your servants Dorogy and others have informed you how much friendship the English have promised to show by acting for you against Danda Rajapore Castle. However, we are ashamed to tell you how much injury we have suffered from them.

Please understand this much: Because we refused to seize the junks lying in the Rajapore River and refused to become enemies to those who were our friends, our broker and one Englishman were carried away by your servants. They were kept and abused in prison for 25 days. Although our broker has now been released, the Englishman is still detained and imprisoned in Carrapatann Castle.

This has caused us great sorrow and has filled all the merchants in the port towns under your command with fear, as they believe this action will harm both their trade and ours.

But we are patient and hope for satisfaction through a letter from you to the chief of these your forces. Therefore, we pray that this letter reaches your hands, and we earnestly request your answer to it regarding the release of our Englishman and the return of whatever else has been taken against your command.

**[E. F. India, 1655-1660, pp. 358-9.]**
