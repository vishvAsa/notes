Ahmad Shah after his victory entered Delhi and sent off letters to the Rajput Rajahs calling upon them to join him with tribute and troops for driving the Marathas out of the empire. [Original farman preserved in Jodhpur.] Madho S. of Jaipur was ordered to present himself with one Kror of Rupees. On receiving this summons, he in alarm appealed to the Peshwa, then in Malwa to come to Bundi, where Madho promised to unite with him after assembling all the other Rajputs. The Peshwa's reply was a stern rebuke for Madho S's coquetting with the anti-Maratha Muslim powers in the past: 

> "You first come to Ajmir taking Bijay S. with you. The Bhau assured you of the possession of, Ranthambhor; he pardoned all your offences at Malhar's intercession. 
> 
> Let bygones be bygone. You are a leading Hindu. In the past you were kindly favoured by us. 
> 
> [But] you relied on him [Najib or Abdali.] Well, ultimately you Rajput Hindus ought to take heed. If the foreigners defeat us, we retire beyond the Narmada; I do not now fear Abdali, [but Rajputana has no escape from him.]" Per. Daf. i. 402 (23 Feb. 1761.)

The Rajput princes of Jaipur and Jodhpur had written to Abdali inviting him to come to India and crush the Marathas. There was considerable correspondence between Abdali and Madho Singh, king of Jaipur, who owed his throne to the Marathas but was bitterly opposed to them. 

The Rajputs did not budge from their domains even when Abdali committed atrocities at Mathura in 1757. In 1757 Raja Keshav rao wrote to the Peshwa,  

> 'The Rajahs of Amber and Jodhpur call themselves Abdali’s servants. They have invited him to be relieved of the Marathas. In return they have agreed to serve him. They will not fight Abdali'.¹⁵
