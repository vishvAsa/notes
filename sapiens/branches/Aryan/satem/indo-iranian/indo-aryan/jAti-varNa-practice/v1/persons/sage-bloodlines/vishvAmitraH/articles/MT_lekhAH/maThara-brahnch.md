A rare example of a shrauta ritualist brAhmaNa of the mAThara gotra, a vaishvAmitra clan from the tamiL country. In the modern tamiL country, mAThara-s are exceedingly rare and largely replaced by the AgamarShaNa-s among the kaushika-s.

> bamhaNasa °ahitagisa ya~naya°isa °ekaba[mha]na[N]Anigapiyasa °ap[A]pasa maDharasago[ta]sa ta[m](i)lasa ma[hA]gAmikasa sAmAput[asa] kaN[h]asa Chayathabho ||

> Memorial pillar of kaNha (kR^iShNa), son of sAmA (shyAmA), mahAgAmika, tamiL, of the mAThara gotra, a brAhmaNa who had set up the (three vedic) fires, who had performed worship, who was the one friend of brAhmaNa-s and learned people (?), (and) who was without sin. -translation Arlo Griffiths.

> "Memorial pillar of Kanha (Kṛṣṇa), son of Sāmā (Śyāmā), mahāgāmikala Tamil, of the Maṭhara gotra, a Brahmin who had set up the (three Vedic) fires, who had performed worship, who was the one friend of Brahmins and learned people (?), (and) who was without sin."

A relatively rare example of a mAThara: the oldest gotra lists mention them as a branch of kaushika-s with vishvAmitra, shaThara and maThara are their pravara R^iShi-s. We also find one on pallava inscription suggesting that they were a branch of pioneer vishvAmitra-s in the peninsular south. andhra-s themselves are said to have emerged from the vishvAmitra-s.


The inscription from Dharanikota shows him in the service of an andhra monarch (not named; sukhAsIna, flanked by two attendents), evidently, whose mahAgAmika he was. Thus, he co-receives the shade of the royal parasol. The memorial is termed a ChAyathabha < ChAyAstambha, the Indo-Aryan term for tamiL neDukal or the hybrid word vIrakal. His title was mahAgAmika < mahAgrAmika, which means he was a provincial administrator of a group of villages for the king. In the andhra and successor states, some officials like mahAgrAmika-s, mahAtalavara-s and mahAraTThi-s minted their own token coins.
