Summary

Shraddhanjali Sabha of Mahamahopadhyaya Shri K. S. Varadacharya

![][image1] From https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o_iNRq897i4

This assembly (Shraddhanjali Sabha) marks the physical departure and celebrates the enduring intellectual and spiritual legacy of Mahamahopadhyaya Shri K. S. Varadacharya (KSV) (1921–2020), a scholar who represented the pinnacle of Ubhaya Vedanta, Nyaya, and Vyakarana in the modern era. Organized by his disciples from Nepal and India, the memorial brought together preeminent heads of monasteries (Mathadhipatis) across sectarian lines, university vice-chancellors, and traditional scholars.

Key Philosophical Themes and Contributions

1. The Hermeneutics of Complementarity (Darsana-Samanvaya)

A central theme of KSV’s life work was the rejection of dogmatic sectarian isolation. In traditional scholarship, Advaita, Visistadvaita, and Dvaita are often taught as mutually destructive systems. KSV proposed a model of dynamic complementarity.

The Layered/Supanic View: He viewed different schools of philosophy as a continuous ladder of spiritual evolution (sopanarupa), where apparent contradictions resolve when mapped to the seeker’s specific stage of consciousness.

2. Grammatical Precision as an Epistemological Filter

KSV utilized Paninian grammar (Vyakarana) not as a dry academic exercise, but as a precise instrument to eliminate semantic noise and uncover the original intent of the Vedic seers. Two major examples were discussed by Dr. M.A. Alwar:

  • The Analysis of Nididhyāsitavyaḥ (Brihadaranyaka Upanisad):
    KSV analyzed the desiderative suffix (san-pratyaya) in the word nididhyāsitavyaḥ (from the root dhyai + san + tavya). He demonstrated that while hearing (sravana) and reflecting (manana) are fully within the domain of human agency (krti), meditation (dhyana) itself is an effortless state of flow dependent on divine grace. Therefore, the Upanisad does not command one to “meditate” (dhyatavyah), but rather to “exert the intense desire and preparation to meditate” (nididhyasitavyah).
  • The Reconstruction of Nidhāya (Tarkasangraha):
    In the opening verse (nidhāya hṛdi viśveśam), the word nidhāya is traditionally translated as “placing.” KSV noted the physical impossibility of placing the omnipresent (vibhu) Lord inside the physical heart. Applying Paninian roots (dhāñ in the sense of dharana-posanayoh), he established that nidhāya here must mean “meditating upon” (dhyatva).

3. The Ontology of the Self (Pratyagartha and Kainkarya)

Sri Madhavapriyadasji Swami and Sri Krishna Kashyap highlighted KSV’s lived metaphysics.

  • Egolessness (Nirahankara): KSV exemplified the transition from the psychological ego (ahankara) to the pure ontological self (pratyagartha). In the liberated state (mukti), the “I” does not dissolve into nothingness; rather, it finds its structural identity as a conscious node of service (kainkarya) to the Whole (Sheshi).
  • Non-Physicality of the Soul: KSV dismantled literalist, physical understandings of the soul’s atomic size (anu). Using the analogy of a “headache” or “happiness”—which are intensely real experiences yet mathematically unmeasurable in centimeters or kilograms—he explained that the atomic nature of the Jivatma refers to its intense qualitative subtlety and spiritual dimension, rather than spatial/physical coordinates.

Biographical and Lineage Highlights

  • Sacred Origins: KSV was born in Kottimangalam Agrahara, a village near Tirukkadalmallai (the birthplace of Bhootathalwar, the second of the Vaishnava Azhwars). The village was historically celebrated as a terrestrial fragment of Vaikuntha due to its high concentration of dedicated Vedic scholars.
  • Lineage of Giants: KSV’s lineage was anchored in the Koushika Gotra, descending from his grandfather Chakravarti Veeraraghavacharya (Kottimangalam Appa). He performed his formal self-surrender (Bhara-samarpana) under him and was initiated into advanced scriptures under the lineage of the Mysore Parakala Matha under Abhinava Ranganatha Brahmatantra Swatantra Parakala Swami.
  • Unbiased Pedagogy: Despite his orthodox Srivaishnava background, KSV taught students of all regions, castes, and sectarian affiliations (including Nimbarkas and Advaitins) without the slightest discrimination. He privately funded the lodging and boarding of impoverished students from Nepal and Northern India to ensure their uninterrupted study of the Ubhaya Vedanta.

Action Plan for the Preservation of His Legacy

The assembly concluded with concrete resolutions to prevent KSV’s vast intellectual output from being lost to entropy:

  1. The Commemorative Volume (Smarana-Grantha):
    Proposed by Sri Gangadharendra Sarasvati Swamiji (Shankaracharya of Swarnavalli Matha), a formal resolution was made to compile and publish a comprehensive commemorative volume. This work will systematically document KSV’s lifestyle, his unique interpretive methodologies, and his biographical milestones, aiming for release on his first annual ceremony (Prathamabdikam).
  2. Digital Preservation and Open Access:
    Led by Sri Krishna Kashyap and the KSV family, a project is underway to digitize and release over 150 recorded audio lectures covering advanced texts like the Jyotir Brahmana. These will be hosted on open-access platforms like archive.org.
  3. Manuscript Publication:
    The Srinivasa Sanskrita Vidyapeetham in Delhi, represented by Sri Jeevan Sharma, offered full institutional and financial support to retrieve, edit, and publish KSV’s remaining unpublished manuscripts, specifically his Satyasiddhih.

00:00 Aakash Nilam To the memory of our preeminent Acharya, Shri K. S. Varadacharya—who was a treasure-house of all scriptures, adorned with many titles such as Pandita-ratna (The Gem among Scholars), and honored by the President of India—in this assembly gathered for his commemoration, we offer our respectful salutations to the Supreme Soul, the Lord of the Universe, who resides in the hearts of all the gathered teachers, disciples, and well-wishers. Victory to Sriman Narayana. Salutations to all.
02:00 Aakash Nilam Recently, such misfortune has befallen us that we are now gathered here in grief to offer flowers of tribute at the auspicious feet of our Acharya through this Zoom technology. Many disciples of the Acharya, who feel fulfilled by his loving grace, have assembled. We are extremely fortunate that Jagadguru Sri Shankaracharya, Sri Gangadharendra Sarasvati Swamiji of Sonda Swarnavalli Matha (Karnataka), has joined to preside over and offer his tributes. Similarly, the divine presence of Jagadguru Ramanujacharya Sri Sridharacharya Swami has graced us from Ayodhya. I now request Sri Jagannathacharya—who is established in all scriptures and is like Agni (fire) in his brilliance—to accept the presidency of this assembly.
04:00 Aakash Nilam …We also have the holy presence of Sri Madhavapriya Dasaji Swami, head of the SGVP Gurukul from Ahmedabad, Gujarat. Acharya B.P. Ramapriya Swami from Melukote, Karnataka, is also present; he will soon narrate the life history of our Acharya. To present the philosophical vision of our Acharya, Sri M.A. Alwar from Mysore is present. Sri Satyaprakash Dasaji Swami from Swaminarayan Mandir, Gujarat, has also joined us to speak on the Acharya’s contributions.
06:00 Aakash Nilam We offer our salutations at their holy feet. Sri Umakanta Bhatta, an outstanding scholar of Nyaya and Vedanta from South India, is also present to pay his tributes. We, the disciples from Nepal, are also gathered. When the news of the Acharya’s departure reached our ears, we wished to travel immediately, but due to the constraints of the COVID pandemic, we were unable to do so physically. However, a few disciples from Nepal have managed to reach the physical site, and through them, we express our devotion.
08:00 Aakash Nilam Our preceptor, Dr. Raghunath Nepal, has organized this event and guided us. Many disciples are present: Prof. Keshavsharan Aryal, Sri Jeevan Sharma (Principal of Shri Srinivasa Vidyapeeth, Delhi), Sri Devanayakacharya (Dinesh Swami) from Vrindavan, Dr. Chakrapani Acharya, Dr. Keshav Luntel, Dr. Yadunandan Ghimire, Prof. Madhav Ghimire, and Upendra Dahal. Since many are joining via mobile, we cannot see everyone at once. I now request Sri Achyuta Prapannacharya Swami and Sri Yadunandan Swami, who have reached the physical venue from Nepal, to kindle the lamp and offer garlands before the portrait of the Acharya. I request Sri Ramapriya Swami to chant the Vedic mangala-charana (auspicious invocations).
09:25 Sri Ramapriya Swami “O Agni (Jatavedas)! Blaze forth, driving away my misfortune. Bring me cattle, long life, and sustenance from all ten directions. Do not harm our cattle, horses, or people, O Jatavedas; come to us with prosperity and protect us.” [Rigveda Khila / Sri Sukta auxiliary prayers] “May the plants that bear flowers, those that bear fruit, and those that are yet without fruit, be victorious like swift horses, helping us cross over difficulties.” [Rigveda 10.97.15 / Yajur Veda] “He understood that Bliss (Ananda) is Brahman. For indeed, from Bliss all these beings are born; by Bliss they live when born; and into Bliss they enter upon departing. This is the wisdom realized by Bhrigu and taught by Varuna, established in the supreme space. He who knows this becomes firmly established.” [Taittiriya Upanisad 3.6.1] Harih Om.
11:02 Aakash Nilam The offering of garlands and the kindling of the lamp before our revered Acharya’s portrait have been completed by Sri Achyuta Prapanna Swami, Sri Yadunandan Swami, and other followers. In Nepal, the intellectual lineage of our Acharya’s teachings is deeply vibrant. Many traveled to Mysore, obtained knowledge, and under his divine guidance, became capable of steering their lives on the path of truth. I now request our preceptor and event coordinator, Dr. Raghunath Nepal, to present the introductory remarks.
12:35 Keshavsharan Aryal Salutations to all the preceptors, scholars, and friends. First, I offer my prostrations at the feet of the revered Acharya: “I bow to Sri Varadacharya, who is proficient in all scriptures, endowed with spiritual qualities, and a revealer of the knowledge of Reality. Endowed with maternal affection and other virtues, a veritable ocean of compassion—I daily bow to Varada, who is Varadacharya. By a mere fraction of whose grace all doubts are severed—to that embodiment of knowledge and experiential wisdom, I bow day and night.”
14:00 Keshavsharan Aryal O respected ones! I offer my salutations to all of you, acknowledging the indwelling Divinity within everyone. I welcome you all with a heart full of gratitude. We all know why we are gathered here. Our Acharya, a storehouse of supreme wisdom, has left this mortal world and departed for Paramapada (the eternal spiritual abode of Sriman Narayana). Though we are deeply grieved, we must find strength in the Acharya’s own teachings: “For one who is born, death is certain” [Bhagavad Gita 2.27] and “Time controls all things.”
16:00 Keshavsharan Aryal It is now our duty to remember the great favor of our Acharya, practice his teachings in our daily lives, and work collectively to publish his writings. For this purpose, we have gathered via Zoom. We are graced by the presence of Sri Gangadharendra Sarasvati Swamiji of the Swarnavalli Matha in North Karnataka, and Jagadguru Sri Shridharacharya Swamiji, the head of the historic Asarpi Bhavan temple of Ayodhya. We also welcome Sri Madhavapriya Swamiji from Gujarat, who has deeply reflected on the Acharya’s works.
18:00 Keshavsharan Aryal Many other scholars are present, including our seniors like Sri Ramapriya Swami, Sri Alwar Swami, and Sri Satyaprasada Swami. Our preceptor, who was in Vrindavan and is now in Devaghat (Nepal), is also present. The greatness of our Acharya was extraordinary; he possessed a thorough, incisive understanding of all scriptures. He did not merely grasp the surface-level subjects but delved deep into their core to bring out the ultimate conclusion (niskarsa). Such scholars are rare today. Many can repeat scriptural texts traditionally, but few can expose the subtle, profound truths like our Acharya did. He blessed everyone who approached him, regardless of where they came from, without any discrimination.
20:00 Dr. Raghunath Nepal “…He had no discrimination whatsoever. He perfectly practiced and lived the Gita verse: ‘The wise look with an equal eye upon a well-behaved Brahmana, a cow, an elephant, a dog, and an outcaste.’ [Bhagavad Gita 5.18]. He guided all toward the path of righteousness and bestowed upon them the knowledge of Truth. Now that our Acharya has departed for Paramapada, it is indeed a time of sorrow. However, we must realize that no one remains here forever. Though he is gone, his teachings and instructions remain with us. Recalling those instructions, we must move forward; that is our only source of strength. I welcome you all again and conclude my words.”
21:55 Aakash Nilam Victory to Sriman Narayana. We have received an illuminating introduction from Dr. Raghunath Nepal. While we are aware of our Acharya’s later scholarship, we are less familiar with his early life and childhood. Knowing this would be highly beneficial for us. I request Sri Ramapriya Swami to shed light on the Acharya’s early life. We also offer our salutations to all the family members, sons, daughters, and relatives of the Acharya.
23:41 Interacting Speakers Aakash Nilam: Is Sri Ramapriya Swamiji present on Zoom, or is there a technical issue? Unidentified Speaker: He is present but muted; he needs to be unmuted. Dr. Raghunath Nepal: Yes, Swamiji, please unmute and speak.
24:03 Sri Ramapriya Swami Salutations. Before I begin, a small request: Pujya Madhavapriyadasji Swami is present, but he has a Srimad Bhagavatam discourse scheduled shortly. Similarly, Sri Gangadharendra Sarasvati Swamiji of Swarnavalli Matha has to perform his evening rituals soon. Therefore, we should request their blessings and messages first. Swamiji from Gujarat was deeply moved by our Acharya’s profound philosophical discourses. Whenever he visited the South, he would spend entire days in KSV’s presence, discussing philosophy. Five years ago, he visited Mysore and honored him. Following the Acharya’s vision, Swamiji has established the Darshanam Sanskrit Mahavidyalaya in Ahmedabad, blending ancient and modern educational methodologies. I request Sri Madhavapriyadasji Swamiji to bless us and offer words of solace to the family. Srimathe Ramanujaya Namah.
27:10 Madhvapriyadasji Swami Om Namo Narayanaya. Respected Ramapriyaji, Nandakumarji, Kulashekharji, Vaijayanthiji, Malini, Kumuda (in spirit), and all members of Gurudev’s family and disciples. I offer my prostrations to all of you. Having lived a full century of life and sanctifying this earth, the news of Guruji’s departure to Vaikuntha has filled our hearts with deep sorrow. It is as if the sun of the philosophical world has set. We were fortunate to have his darshana and hear his nectar-like words. He was the personification of Vedanta; Visistadvaita Vedanta was in his every pore. He was the glory of India, which is why the President of India honored him. Yet, I felt that award was too small for his immense stature. Guruji was completely detached from titles and honors; his true honor lay in serving the feet of Sriman Narayana. There are many scholars and ascetics, but just as a river is incomplete until it meets the ocean, all knowledge, penance, and renunciation must merge into the ocean of devotion (bhakti). Guruji’s entire life was an act of worship (upasana). Without upasana, the grace of Lord Narayana cannot be obtained. In our Vaishnava tradition, the highest ideal is Kainkarya (loving service). Guruji, despite his towering intellect, lived as a humble servant (kinkar). The greatest obstacle on the spiritual path is the subtle ego—the ego of being knowledgeable, virtuous, or detached. Guruji was a completely egoless personality. In the state of liberation (mukti), the individual “I” does not vanish; it remains as the servant of the Lord: aham bhagavataha dasosmi (“I am the servant of the Lord”). This Kainkarya-bhava was visible in Guruji. When he reached Vaikuntha, surely the Divine Mother Lakshmi must have told Lord Narayana: “Lord, Your child has arrived,” and the liberated souls (muktas) must have welcomed him. This is the fulfillment of life—to belong to Him. On behalf of the entire SGVP Gurukul family, I offer my tribute. He has left an immense void in the fields of knowledge and devotion. I pray that Lord Narayana sends such souls again to guide us. My respects to the family and to all.
36:31 Aakash Nilam Salutations.
36:35 Sri Ramapriya Swami “He belonged to Him, and He took him back.” Swamiji has beautifully blessed the family and disciples. We are highly grateful to him. Dr. Raghunatha-acharya-varya has assigned me the task of presenting the Acharya’s life history. In reality, the Acharya’s life was an open book. He was an embodiment of the verse: ‘The wise look with an equal eye upon a Brahmana and an outcaste’ [Bhagavad Gita 5.18]. I recall an incident from his life: a cat used to visit him daily. He would carefully save his own portion of milk to feed the cat. Sri Alwar’s mother, Godamba, noticed this and said: “O Acharya! This milk is for you, but since you are so compassionate to all living beings, from tomorrow I will send an extra half-liter of milk for the cat.” This shows the tenderness of his heart. I will now share details of his life. “I take refuge in the twin feet of the Supreme Person, the preceptor of all, by whose grace I am freed from the bondage of bodily existence.” Srimathe Ramanujaya Namah. Salutations to the lineage of preceptors.
41:35 Sri Ramapriya Swami Our revered Acharya, Pandita-ratnam Varadacharya Swami, was born before India’s independence in the holy land of Kanchipuram, near Tirukkadalmallai (modern-day Mahabalipuram)—the birthplace of Bhootathalwar (one of the early Azhwars). His ancestral village was Kottimangalam Agrahara, a place of devout Vedic scholars, of whom it was said: “This is not a mere worldly village, but a piece of Vaikuntha descended on earth.” His father was Sundararajacharya, an expert in Nyaya, Vyakarana, Vedanta, and Sahitya, whose father was Krishnacharya. His grandfather was the celebrated Chakravarti Veeraraghavacharya (known affectionately as Kottimangalam Appa), under whose feet our Acharya performed bhara-samarpana (the formal ritual of self-surrender/Saranagati in the Srivaishnava tradition). His initiation (samashrayanam) was performed by his uncle Gopalappa, a giant of scholarship known as the “Second Vyasa” of the village.
49:20 Sri Ramapriya Swami …Our Acharya never indulged in self-praise (svashlagha). He never spoke about his family, his lineage, or his achievements to his disciples. Whenever a student approached, he immediately dove into the subject matter, tailoring the instruction to the student’s capacity. He once told us that during his youth, he would walk around the village constantly muttering and contemplating the rules of Sabadabodha (verbal cognition and semantics). The local vegetable and curd vendors would look at him and say, “This boy has gone mad, he keeps talking to himself!” Such was his absorption in study. His early Vedic studies were completed in his village under the Somayajin Narayana Tatacharya. Later, to study advanced treatises of Navyanyaya like Panchalakshani and Chaturdashalakshani, he went to the Sri Aadikeshavalu Shettiyar Sanskrit College in Chennai, and later to Tirupati, where his classmate was the famous Velukkudi Varadacharya (father of Sri Velukkudi Krishnan).
54:32 Sri Ramapriya Swami …A classmate’s testimony is the most genuine proof of one’s scholarship. Velukkudi Varadacharya wrote in a commemorative volume that if one wants to see the physical embodiment of the Upanisadic statement: “Having transcended scholarship, one should live like a child” [Brihadaranyaka Upanisad 3.5.1], one must look at K. S. Varadacharya. To study even more advanced texts, he traveled to the Mysore Parakala Matha and sought the shelter of Abhinava Ranganatha Brahmatantra Swatantra Parakala Swami, a towering spiritual personality of the era.
55:38 Sri Ramapriya Swami …Under the guidance of the Parakala Swamiji, KSV studied advanced texts of Vedanta and Nyaya. Recognizing his brilliance, the Parakala Swamiji directed his own disciple, Atmakuru Deekshacharyavaryah, to teach him further. Though KSV lived at the Parakala Matha (near the city bus stand), he would daily walk barefoot all the way to Krishnamurthypuram to Deekshacharya’s house for classes, continuously contemplating philosophical arguments along the way. He spent nearly ten years in intensive study, mastering all six systems of Indian philosophy (Saddarsana). He also served as a teacher at the Maharaja’s Sanskrit College, Mysore.
58:00 Sri Ramapriya Swami Later, he was appointed as a research head at the Oriental Research Institute (ORI) in Mysore, which houses over a hundred thousand palm-leaf manuscripts. There, he edited and wrote a brilliant commentary (tika) on Jayanta Bhatta’s classical logical treatise, the Nyayamanjari. He was only 31 or 32 years old then, yet his extraordinary scholarship caught the attention of the Sage of Kanchi, Sri Chandrasekharendra Saraswathi (the Mahaperiyava), who bestowed upon him the title Pandita-ratna (The Gem of Scholars). Later, in 2002, he received the Certificate of Honor from the President of India, and was decorated with the title Mahamahopadhyaya by the National Sanskrit University, Tirupati. He had three sons: Sri Ramapriya, Sri Nandakumar, and Sri Yadusekhara, and three daughters: Kumuda (who pre-deceased him), Vaijayanti, and Malini. He was an untiring teacher; during his time in Melukote, he would teach up to sixteen hours a day without any break. He taught scholars, ascetics, and householders alike. I am but a humble recipient of a tiny fraction of his grace. KSV did not approach this by asserting one as completely authoritative and others as completely false. He investigated how close each commentary is to the truth (satyasya nikatavarti). He taught that ultimate truth is beyond mere words; it is accessible only through penance (tapas): “Seek to know Brahman through penance.” [Taittiriya Upanisad 3.8.1]. Yet, words are necessary for communication. He spent sixty years analyzing which aspects of each Bhasya are robust and where they fall short. 1:38:00 Speaker 4 (M.A. Alwar) …Another profound contribution of KSV is the distinction between Jnana (conceptual knowledge) and Vijnana (experiential realization). Similarly, he clarified the distinction between Rta (cosmic/moral order) and Satya (factual truth), showing how the ignorance of this distinction has led to many philosophical errors in the scriptures. Let us look at the famous Upanisadic passage: “The Self, my dear, should be seen, heard of, reflected upon, and meditated upon.” [Brihadaranyaka Upanisad 2.4.5]. KSV explained that while hearing (sravana) and reflecting (manana) are within human control, meditation (dhyana) is not entirely under individual control; it depends on divine grace. May all the virtues taught in the Upanisads reside in me, who am devoted to the Self. Om Peace, Peace, Peace.” [Chandogya Upanisad, Invocation]. “May all be happy, may all be free from illness. May all see what is auspicious, may no one suffer.” [Traditional Upanisadic prayer]. Srimathe Ramanujaya Namah. Thank you all for your patient, joyful participation. We offer our respects to the family of the Acharya. Srimathe Ramanujaya Namah.
