A little later on, in the early Edo period, junshi (following one’s lord in death) became a huge craze.

When a famous daimyo died, about 20 people would commit junshi. Even the people who performed the kaishaku (beheading to end suffering) for those junshi victims would themselves commit junshi, slitting their bellies to follow suit.

Amid all that, the Nabeshima clan was brutal—in addition to the retainers committing junshi, nearly 20 of the retainers’ own retainers would follow them into death.

**It wasn’t just men dying; when the lady of the Nabeshima clan passed away, the female attendants who served her also committed junshi (with their husbands probably performing the kaishaku, and then the husbands committing junshi afterward).**

Whether women performed kaishaku is unknown, but it’s a fact that there were some seriously committed women back then.

By the way, in the Book of Five Rings, Musashi says something like how it’s crucial for samurai to have resolve to die—but then he flatly states that even women and children can have that kind of resolve and emphasizes that resolve to win is what’s truly important, which really captures the era’s casual disregard for life, or whatever you wanna call it.

On a side note, if a daimyo lacked personal virtue, the number of junshi would drop, and the taciturn, communication-averse Uesugi Kagekatsu only had about two people follow him in death.

Conversely, when Hosokawa Tadamasu—the ultimate social butterfly—died, there were around 20 junshi, including low-ranking folks with zero apparent connection to him. When asked beforehand why, they apparently said it was because Tadamasu had casually chatted with them at some point.

Tadamasu was one of the all-time good guys among daimyo, but it’s ironic how that ended up being his downfall…

That was just something that crossed my mind while looking at the source material.
