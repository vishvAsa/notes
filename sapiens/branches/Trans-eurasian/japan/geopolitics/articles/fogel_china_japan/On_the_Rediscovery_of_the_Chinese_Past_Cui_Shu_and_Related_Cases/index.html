What is meant by the ‚Äúrediscovery‚Äù of some old text, ancient heritage, or person from another era? What is especially interesting about the case of Cui Shu is that his rediscovery occurred twice‚Äîonce in China and once in Japan‚Äîunder entirely different circumstances, and it therefore provides us with a fascinating case of comparative sinology. For nearly half a century the historiographical output of the Republican period has been shielded from critical evaluation by two connecting forces: (1) a pervasive nationalism, which among other things has taught students of modern Chinese history that all foreign powers and Japan in particular were monstrous ogres at virtually every turn (this can greatly distort one‚Äôs perception of events in twentieth-century history and bring an emotional charge to terms like ‚Äúimperialist‚Äù), and (2) the research of a handful of men, like Hu Shi (1891‚Äì1962) and his group of students, whose overwhelming intellects have made serious criticism of their work, or in fact their whole intellectual project, extremely difficult.
