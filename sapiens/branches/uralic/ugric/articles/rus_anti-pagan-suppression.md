## Kazan fall
Both Finno-Ugric pagans (Mari, Udmurt, Mordvins) and Muslim Tatars were forcefully baptism after Ivan the Terrible conquered the Kazan Khanate.

Any pagans who resisted suffered destruction of their sacred groves and the seizure of their lands.

When Kazan fell, the city was leveled and the contemporary chronicles even describe the Volga River running red.

The current Qolşärif Mosque in Kazan in Tatarstan is actually a very recent construction (2005 AD), the original structure being destroyed in 1552.

In the 18th century, Empress Elizabeth Petrovna even established the Kontora Novokreshchenykh (Office of the Newly Baptized).

Anyone who was caught practicing pagan rites after being baptized was treated as an apostate and the penalty for apostasy was imprisonment, torture, or exile to Siberia.

During this period, the Russian Orthodox Church also burnt down thousands of sacred groves/küsoto and built churches directly on top of ancient pagan sacrificial sites with the goal of "stifling" the old spirits.

## mari
Some Mari fought back against the Russians up until the 19th century and when military resistance proved futile they moved deeper into the forests to maintain their rites in secret.

It was the buffer of the dense taiga and the vast Eurasian steppe that protected them.

It was not until Catherine the Great that the policy shifted toward greater religious tolerance, but even that tolerance was mainly towards Muslims (e.g. the establishment of the Orenburg Muslim Spiritual Assembly).
