Many Sámi were also force-converted, but it was mainly by Scandinavian Lutheran missionaries and not by the Russian Orthodox Church (unless you count the Sámi of the Kola Peninsula)

The Lutheran missionaries and state officials often toppled or defaced sieidis (sacred stone or wooden objects) and confiscated and burned thousands of goavddis/goabdes (shamanistic drums).

Possession of a goavddis drum was regarded as witchcraft in Tvillingrigerne (The Twin Kingdoms, i.e. Denmark and Norway), so Sámi spiritual leaders/shamans (i.e. Noaidi) were often prosecuted in witch trials. 

One of the most famous trials of a Sámi shaman was Anders Poulsson in 1692. It was likely that Poulsson would be acquited due to lack of evidence, but Anders was unfortunately killed prior to sentencing by a Christian servant boy who struck Poulsson in the head with an axe because he was terrified that the shaman would use his "magic" to harm him or others.


## Finn connection
Also related to the topic of Anders Poulsson and his famous drum, the Sámi have a very archaic reputation of sorcery/magic (and also skiing), e.g. En er seiðr var framiðr, þá var Vanlandi at Uppsölum; þá gerði hann fúsan at fara til Finnlands, en vinir hans ok ráðamenn bönnuðu honum, ok sögðu at vera mundi fjölkyngi Finna í farfýsi hans. (Ynglinga Saga)

> "But when the spell (seiðr) had been performed, Vanlandi was at Uppsala; then he became eager to travel to Finnland, but his friends and counselors forbade it, saying that the sorcery of the Sámi must be behind his sudden urge to go."

Note that the word used in Old Norse for a Sámi person is Finnr or Fiðr. Although the exact etymology of Finnr is debated, some scholars have posited that the Germanic farmers of Scandinavia referred to the Sámi as "Finns" because they were "finders" who hunted & gathered their food instead of growing their food.

The Norsemen used the Old Norse terms Finnland and Finnmörk [English Finnmark] to refer to the "Land of the Sámi," i.e. the northernmost part of Norway that borders Russia. When the sagas say that a king went to Finnmörk to find a "Finn" (Sámi) sorcerer, it indicates that they are going to the Arctic NOT Finland.

The ancestors of the people we call "Finns" today are actually the "Kvenir" of the Old Norse Sagas who lived around the Gulf of Bothnia and not the Old Norse Finnar. The English usage of "Finland" actually comes from the Swedes.

The Swedish settlers originally used the term Finland (finlonti or finlandi) for Varsinais-Suomi/Egentliga Finland (i.e. "Finland proper," the region of centered around the city of Turku).

As Sweden expanded its rule across the region, the name "Finland" was eventually applied to the entire country. 

Interestingly, the Finns and the Sámi do likely stem from a common ancestral group ~3000 years ago. Even today, the Finns call their land Suomi, which is likely a linguistic sibling with Sámi/Sápmi/Sáme. Both terms might possibly derive from an early Baltic loanword *źemē meaning "land" or "earth."

The people who stayed closer to the coast, became more settled, and intermixed heavily with the Balts and Scandinavians/Swedes became the modern Suomi (or Finns).

The group that maintained a more nomadic, inland hunter-gatherer lifestyle specialized to the Arctic environment (reindeer herding, fishing, etc.) and  preserved more of their Siberian/Nganasan-related genetic signature became the Sámi (Sámi are ~25% East Eurasian while Finns are ~5% East Eurasian).
