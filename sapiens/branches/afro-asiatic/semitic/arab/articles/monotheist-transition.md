<details><summary>Gemini Summary</summary>

The paper "Late Antique Allāh: Ancestral Arabian Religion and the Monotheistic Zeitgeist" by Ahmad Al-Jallad and Hythem Sidky proposes a new synthesis to resolve the conflict between two historical views: the traditional Islamic view of "rampant polytheism" in pre-Islamic Arabia and the modern scholarly view that pre-Islamic Arabs were "imperfect monotheists."

The findings can be summarized into four key points:

**1. The "Monotheistic Zeitgeist"**

Based on a study of over 40 recently discovered **Paleo-Arabic inscriptions** from the 6th century CE, the authors find that Arabian religious language had already undergone a revolution before Islam. 

*   In earlier centuries, inscriptions mentioned a massive pantheon of gods (Allāt, Dusares, etc.).
*   By the 6th century, **all other gods had vanished from the public epigraphic record.** 
*   Inscriptions from this era invoke **only one deity** (Allāh or al-ʾilāh) and use language nearly identical to later Islamic formulas (e.g., seeking forgiveness, enjoining piety).

**2. Allāh as an Ancestral Creator God**

The authors argue that Allāh was not a new god introduced by the Quran. Instead, he was an **ancient Arabian creator deity** who, due to the cultural prestige of surrounding monotheistic empires (Rome and Ḥimyar), was increasingly identified with the God of the Bible. By the time of Mohammed, Allāh had already become the "supreme, universal God" in the public consciousness of North and West Arabia.

**3. Reconciling the Quran with the Inscriptions**

If inscriptions only mention Allāh, why does the Quran attack the worship of other entities (the *mušrikīn*)? The authors propose:

*   **The "Goddesses" as Angels:** Entities like Allāt, al-ʿUzzē, and Manōh had been "theologically demoted." They were no longer viewed as autonomous, powerful gods, but as **subordinate intercessors or angels** (daughters of Allāh). 
*   **Public vs. Private:** Because they were seen as lesser beings, they disappeared from "formal" public inscriptions (which were reserved for the supreme creator) but remained in "folk religion" and oral tradition, which is what the Quran addresses.

**4. Redefining the "Idols" of the Ǧāhiliyyah**

The authors suggest that the "rampant idol worship" described in later Islamic literature (like Ibn al-Kalbī’s *Book of Idols*) has been misunderstood by later historians:

*   The "idols" mentioned in later memories were often **superstitious folk objects** (sacred rocks, trees, or rain-making effigies like *umm al-ġayṯ*) rather than members of a functional mythological pantheon.
*   Later Muslim writers, living in a strictly monotheistic world, conflated these "superstitious objects" with "gods" to create a literary image of the *Ǧāhiliyyah* (the Age of Ignorance) that seemed more polytheistic than it actually was.

**Conclusion**  
The paper concludes that **Mohammed did not introduce a new god.** Instead, he was a **reformer** of an existing "imperfect monotheism." He sought to "biblicize" a system where Allāh was already the supreme deity but where people still relied on intercessors and folk superstitions. His mission was to purge these remnants and align Arabian practice with strict monotheistic standards.

## ABSTRACT
This essay addresses the ongoing scholarly tension between the monotheistic interpretations of late pre-Islamic Arabian religion, pioneered by G. Hawting and P. Crone, and the traditional accounts of rampant Arabian polytheism found in later Islamic literary sources. By integrating the analysis of the Quran and Islamic literary sources with the rapidly growing body of Paleo-Arabic epigraphy from Late Antique West Arabia, the authors propose a synthesis that resolves this apparent contradiction.

---

## 1 | Introduction

With his seminal monograph, *The Idea of Idolatry*, G. Hawting pioneered a new approach to understanding the religious context of the Quran and its primary antagonists, the *mušrikīn*.[^1] A close analysis of the portrayal of pre-Islamic Arabian ‘pagans’ in later Muslim literary sources led him to conclude that they bore little resemblance to Mohammed’s Meccan interlocutors. Rather, the entire drama between the Quranic protagonist and his opponents was said to come down to the details: the *mušrikūn* were simply imperfect monotheists. Patricia Crone refined this theory in several important contributions, highlighting the similarities between the religious climate in early seventh-century Mecca and the Arabian tribespeople ʿAbdu l-Wahhāb condemned.[^2] Her final essay on this matter understood the pagan Arabs as God-Fearers. ‘Pagan monotheism’ was not an isolated Arabian peculiarity;[^3] the broader religious landscape of Late Antiquity featured a significant presence of ‘God-Fearers’ (θεοσεβεῖς): individuals and groups across the Mediterranean and Near East devoted to a supreme deity without full conversion to Judaism or Christianity.[^4] Reading the Quran in this light contextualizes late pre-Islamic West Arabian beliefs within the religious currents of late antiquity. In other words, West Arabia partook in the ‘monotheistic Zeitgeist’, which transformed the religious consciousness of the era. By the sixth century, monotheistic concepts rooted in the culture of the Bible had spread widely throughout the Ḥiǧāz, leading to a syncretism between ancestral Arabian religion and Judaism, and, eventually, Christianity.

Although this new interpretation of Mohammed’s environment arose from a careful consideration of evidence found in the Quran and later accounts, it raises two significant and as-of-yet unresolved problems—(1) What are we to make of the entities the *mušrikūn* venerated, mentioned in Q 53:19-20? and (2) What of the accounts of rampant idol worship described in later sources? Are they to be understood as purely fictions of the later Muslim imagination? Crone’s solution to conundrum 1 was to regard the three ‘goddesses’, Allāt, al-ʿUzzē and Manōh, as angels or minor gods, an inference based on a close reading of sūrah 53 and the satanic verses episode. In both cases, these three female ‘superbeings’ were subordinate to the creator. Hawting held this view as well, but his understanding was that the figures represented a reinterpretation of older Arabian gods, now seen as angels within a biblical framework. Both readings lead to the same conclusion regarding conundrum 2: the Ǧāhiliyyah of later sources was largely fictional.

This line of reconstruction has met considerable resistance. M. Lecker responded to Hawting’s theory by drawing on evidence from ‘conversion stories’ attributed to Mohammed’s companions, arguing that these accounts, while mostly fictional in their details, still attested to rampant idol worship across the Peninsula.[^5] And that these physical idols, rather than abstract supernatural entities, were a major point of contention between Mohammed’s movement and surrounding groups.[^6] Al-Azmeh regards such reinterpretations of the Quranic milieu as a symptom of a larger phenomenon of hyperskepticism, which results in the wholesale, yet unjustified, dismissal of later Arabic sources.[^7] He goes on to maintain that the historian’s first port of call to understand the Quran’s context and audience should be Islamic-period Arabic sources. For him, Mohammed’s Arabia was a pagan environment largely untouched by the monotheistic currents of surrounding societies. The monotheistic reading of the text by Hawting, Crone, Sinai et al. comes from the anachronistic interpretation of epithetic nomenclatures as reflecting coherent theologies, a folly that produces a featureless, and ultimately unrealistic, ‘pagan’ monotheism.[^8]

## 2 | The Epigraphy of Late Antique West Arabia

The works mentioned above predate the ‘material turn’ in the study of late Pre-Islamic Arabia. Their conclusions, as such, are based on the subjective weighing of conflicting sources. For example, Crone et al. do not consider the conversion narratives that Lecker uses to be reliable sources for the Quranic milieu, and there is little to arbitrate between both opinions, as Al-Azmeh’s rebuttals reveal. Moreover, the discourse relies too heavily on many double-barred terms and concepts, the meaning of which remain contentious, such as ‘monotheism’ and ‘god’. Few works spell out what features these concepts are supposed to entail and what they correspond to in the idiom of the Quran and early literature concerning the Ǧāhiliyyah.

Over the past decade, the epigraphic exploration of Arabia Deserta has advanced significantly. When Hawting wrote his book, there were only three Arabic inscriptions dating to the sixth century CE, and all three came from the Levant.[^9] These texts were clearly Christian and could not be used to draw conclusions about Meccan society at the time. The absence of material evidence from the Ḥiǧāz even led some to question whether the region was substantially inhabited before the rise of Islam.[^10] In 2014, the Franco-Saudi mission to Naǧrān published a number of Paleo-Arabic texts from the region of Ḥimà, north of the city.[^11] These inscriptions demonstrate the use of the Arabic script within the Arabian Peninsula by the late fifth century CE (the earliest dates to 470 CE) and also attest to the spread of Christianity as far as the Yemeni frontier. In these writings, the name of god is *al-ʾilāh*, a clear calque from the Greek *ho theós* and/or Aramaic *alāhā*.[^12] While the presence of Christianity in Naǧrān is not unexpected, the following years witnessed the documentation of sixth century Paleo-Arabic texts from across the western half of the Peninsula, including Dūmat al-Jandal, the region of Tabūk, Medina and Ṭāʾif,[^13] regions that traditionally were thought to have a majority ‘pagan’ population.

Arabian inscriptions from classical antiquity and earlier—whether they be in the Nabataean script or carved in the various Ancient North Arabian alphabets—contain copious references to the old gods. They form our primary source for understanding ancestral Arabian cultic practices and language.[^14]

In contrast, the Paleo-Arabic inscriptions, which date to about a century before Islam, stand apart. Now, with more than 40 documented Paleo-Arabic texts from across Arabia, it can no longer be considered a coincidence that only one deity, allāh/al-ʾilāh, is invoked in language that is virtually identical to Quranic and later Islamic diction. There is no trace of the veneration of the old gods, who were abundantly mentioned in earlier corpora from the same regions, in the sixth century Arabic inscriptions known so far.[^15] Explanations suggesting that the only literate individuals were monotheists are untenable, as we have abundant textual evidence of polytheistic worship dating to periods before this era—clearly, something fundamental has changed.

Thus, the corpus as it stands is not completely congruent with later religious cartography or the Quran as traditionally understood. The centrality of the other deities would imply their frequent appearance in invocations, oaths, and other contexts, just as they are attested in the ancient North and South Arabian inscriptions before the fourth century CE. When put into this context, the Paleo-Arabic inscriptions reflect a revolution in invocational language—Allāh (or al-ʾilāh), who was comparatively rarely called upon before, is now the sole object of veneration, while the other gods who populated the ancient inscriptions, have disappeared from invocational formulae altogether. In addition to this, there emerges a number of new invocational categories that are unattested in the ancient Arabian inscriptions: enjoining piety; blessing; and seeking forgiveness.[^16] To illustrate this phenomenon, consider the following pre-fourth century CE texts from Northwest Arabia:

**Nabataean (Ḥegrā, first c. CE)**[^17]
‘… And may Dusares and his throne and Allāt from *mnw* and Manōto (=Manāh) and her scales curse anyone who would sell this tomb…’

**Hismaic (Ḫašm al-Ṣanaʿ, between Tabūk and al-ʿUlā, pre-fourth century CE)**[^18]
‘By {S¹ʿdlh} son of {Brd} of the lineage of Wly; and may Allāt be mindful of the lineage of Wly’

**Dadanitic (Al-ʿUlā, latter half of the first millennium BCE)**[^19]
‘S¹s¹n priestess of Ḏū-Ġābat (god of Dadān) organized the ẓll-ceremony for the sake of {her} palm trees in Ḏʿmn and so may he (=the god Ḏū-Ġābat) favor her and help her and reward her and her posterity’

Now, from the same region (West Arabia), observe the contents of the Paleo-Arabic texts from the sixth century CE:

**NRAA 4 (Umm Burayrah, west of Tabūk):**[^20]
بسمك اللهم انا عبدشمس بر المغيره يستغفر ربه
‘In your name, O Allāh, I am ʿAbd-šams son of ʾal-Muġīrah, who seeks the forgiveness of his Lord’

**FaS 7 (Jabal ʿAyrayn, south of Tabūk):**[^21]
من خلد بر ثعلبه لب<ن>ي لخزرج سلم انتم فاذا اتيتم علي مكن هذا فلاوصكم بله
‘From Ḫālid son of Ṯaʿlabah to the Ḫazraǧites; may you be at peace and when you come to this place of mine, let me urge you (to obey) Allāh’

**JPICM 2 (Ṭāʾif):**[^22]
بسمك ربنا انا عبدالعزي بر سفين اوصي ببر الله
‘In your name, our lord, I am ʿAbd al-ʿUzzē son of Sufyān; I enjoin piety towards Allāh’

This basic observation, which has only been possible in the last few years, requires an explanation. The only systematic attempt to reconcile the apparent discrepancy between Islamic sources and the epigraphic record that has appeared so far is found in Lindstedt’s book, *Muhammad and his Followers in Context* (2023). There, he understands the epigraphic evidence to support Crone’s interpretation of the Meccan population as ‘God-Fearers’, but he applies this to the entirety of the Arabian Peninsula, arguing that the majority of Arabians were either Christians or Jews, or gentile heno- or monotheists.[^23] He considers the epigraphic record and the testimony of the Ǧāhilī odes, as interpreted by Sinai,[^24] to constitute positive evidence against Lecker’s position regarding the persistence of polytheism,[^25] but hedges this assertion by stating that epigraphic and archaeological research around Mecca and Medina ‘has been nonexistent’.[^26] Lindstedt envisions an Arabia that consists of three primary categories of religiosity: Jews, Christians and ‘Gentile Monotheists’. The final group, however, is poorly defined, an anachronism of his own making. In what sense they are to be regarded as monotheists? What does being a ‘gentile monotheist’ even entail in sixth-century Arabia? Lindstedt concedes that sacrificing at cult stones may have persisted and that they rejected the notion of post-mortem judgement and the afterlife, yet this derives entirely from the Quran and not the inscriptional record. We are given no decisive methodological criteria to prefer one set of data over the other. While the Paleo-Arabic inscriptions clearly document a change in what is expressed epigraphically, Lindstedt risks taking for granted that the inscriptions provide us with the complete worldview of their authors by declaring on their basis that Arabia was ‘monotheistic’. But does the invocation of a single deity in public graffiti necessarily equate to a rejection of all other divine beings? We think not. In our judgement, the category of ‘gentile monotheist’ is of questionable explanatory value in the present context. If this group denied the resurrection, continued to sacrifice at cult stones, and indeed venerate other superbeings, how are they substantially different from the traditional conceptualization of the Quranic antagonists in the first place?

We are therefore brought back to the drawing board. There are three categories of evidence that to this point do not easily fit together to form a coherent narrative (Table 1):

To put the epigraphic timeline into context, we should give some attention to the religious transformations heralded by the domination of Ḥimyar in South Arabia. Based on the material evidence, South Arabia experienced a religious reformation in the fourh century CE, resulting in the abandonment of ancestral cults in the official realm and the introduction of biblically inspired monotheism.[^27] Robin has argued that this conversion was top down, first affecting the Himyarite elites and thereafter proliferating to the lower sections of society in an organic way.[^28] The main shift in cosmology entailed worship centered on one god, the all-powerful creator and sovereign of the heavens and the earth.

While the process of monotheiziation may have been top down in Yemen, its effects diffused organically far beyond areas directly under the control of Ḥimyar. In this same period, we begin to witness the recession of pagan invocations in the inscriptions throughout Arabia.[^29] Remarkably, the Namarah inscription, from the beginning of the fourth century, is entirely secular; it commemorates the life of the king of all the Arabs and his *res gestae*, but unlike earlier Nabataean inscriptions, it terminates without any invocation or mention of a god.[^30] Even the Thamudic D inscriptions of Northwest Arabia, which can now be dated to at least between the third and fifth centuries CE, lack any invocations or references to the old gods.[^31] In fact, the only deity that can be associated with this corpus comes from the well-known Thamudic D inscription, JSTham 1, associated with the late Nabataean tomb inscription JSNab 17 (Jaussen and Savignac 1920) at Hegra, dated to 267 CE. The Nabataean text invokes only one superbeing, *mry ʿlmʾ* (‘lord of eternity’), which is an established epithet of the Jewish god. So while ‘pagan’ names persist in the inscriptions of late antiquity, the inscriptions of this period qualitatively differ from the epigraphic traditions of antiquity in their mundane nature.

Because these texts lack religious invocations, we cannot say anything about the theological identity of their inscribers. The absence of the ancestral Arabian gods may be interpreted as a decline in paganism or simply a shift in epigraphic habits under the pressure of the taboo on paganism in the adjacent empires of Rome and Ḥimyar.[^32] It is in this light that the nature of inscriptions of the Paleo-Arabic period (late fifth to early seventh c. CE) becomes all the more distinct. After nearly three centuries of mundane texts, religious invocations and symbols return once again to the inscriptions of Arabia in full force. In Ḥimà, north of Najrān, Dūmat al-Jandal and the Levant, the rise of religious language can be attributed to Christianity—many inscriptions contain overt symbols of Christianity, including crosses. They make use of a limited set of compositional formula, the commonest of which is *ḏkr ʾl-ʾlh PN* ‘may the God be mindful of PN’, the inscriptions are sometimes dated using the Roman province of Arabia, and the monotheistic god is consistently called *al-ʾilāh*.[^33]

In order to appreciate the significance of this fact, let us observe that the South Arabians did not repurpose an earlier divine name like *ʾilumqahu*, *Wadd*, or *ʿAṯtar* for the biblical/monotheistic god. Rather, neutral epithets like ‘master of the heaven and earth’, calques, *ʾl(h)n* ‘the god’, or straightforward borrowings, *rḥmnn* ‘the merciful’, accomplished this.[^34] Thus, the Christian Paleo-Arabic texts, available at present, appear to follow this pattern by calquing Aramaic *alāhā* and Greek *ho theós* into Arabic as *al-ʾilāh*, literally ‘the god’.[^35]

The matter becomes more complicated once we look to West Arabia, the Ḥiǧāz. The Paleo-Arabic inscriptions continue the trend of monotheistic expressions, but they differ qualitatively from the Christian inscriptions of this category. For one, they do not contain crosses or other forms of religious iconism. They do not make use of the *ḏkr ʾl-ʾlh* formula nor are they dated using the Roman era of Arabia. And, perhaps most importantly, they do not call upon *ʾl-ʾlh* ‘the God’.[^36] Rather they invoke allāh, spelled in various ways: *ʾlh*, *ylh*, and *ʾllh*.[^37] This distribution cannot be considered coincidental. Speaking only in terms of content, the Ḥiǧāzī texts are also strictly monotheistic in that they invoke only one deity. And they do so in terms that are completely unattested in earlier Arabian material but, rather, anticipate Islamic conventions. Departures from ancient diction, including the urging the passerby to be pious towards Allāh and asking Allāh to pardon the author and his community, are themes that fit best within the religious ideals of Christianity, proselytization and penance, respectively, suggesting a religious climate and culture, if not Christian, deeply impacted by Christianity.[^38]

The ‘God-Fearer’ (*theosebeis*) phenomenon of the late antique Mediterranean and Near East offers a compelling framework for understanding the nature of the Ḥiǧāzī inscriptions. The God-Fearers were individuals or groups who worshipped a supreme deity and adhered to aspects of monotheistic piety without undertaking full conversion to Judaism or Christianity. Their religious expressions often lacked the rigid doctrinal frameworks, established prophetic lineages, or ritual-legal systems of the Jewish or Christian traditions.[^39] Instead, they could be characterized by a looser adherence to core monotheistic concepts, sometimes incorporating eclectic practices or local traditions within a broadly monotheistic outlook. Robin suggested that this characterization best captures the religious identity of the South Arabian monotheists who did not fully convert to Judaism.[^40]

Such a model can help us understand the religious transformations occurring in West Arabia in the century prior to Mohammed’s ministry as well. Ties not only to the Roman empire but also to Ḥimyar in the south gave prestige to a religious ideology centered on the worship of the creator as the supreme power in the universe and fostered an atmosphere of proselytization. But the Palaeo-Arabic situation still differs in an important way from the Ḥimyarite inscriptions. While South Arabian Christians and Jews both called their god by the same name, *raḥmānān*, North Arabian nomenclature for the one god differed based on confession—Christians called god *al-ʾilāh* while texts lacking overt Christian features call upon god with the name *allāh*. While this may seem at first like a simple, dialectal matter, and indeed the two names share an etymology (*al + ʾilāh*),[^41] the situation is made complicated by the fact that allāh is attested as the proper name of a deity in the inscriptions of pre-Islamic Arabia in contexts where it is not etymologically analysable as ‘the god’.[^42]

In 2020, C. Robin established that Allāh was the name of a specific Arabian deity, rather than simply a generic appellative, whose veneration dates back to the latter half of the first millennium BCE at the latest.[^43] However, the texts available to Robin at the time provided little to no mythological information; Allāh’s role in the cosmos remained obscure. In 2025, Al-Jallad published a newly discovered Safaitic inscription, which, when read along with other known texts, has helped bring his character into focus. In North Arabia at least, Allāh was regarded as a living (*ḥy*) creator (*ḫlq*) who gives light (*nr*) and who guides (*ḫfrt*).[^44]

As Al-Azmeh has astutely put it: polytheism is syncretising. But what happens when ancestral polytheistic cults encounter a mythological framework that does not accommodate a plurality of superbeings possessing autonomous power in the world? The authors of the Safaitic inscriptions, while understanding Allāh to be the *ḫlq* ‘creator’, still called upon distinct personalities to affect human affairs. If inscriptional frequency is any indicator, Allāh was a rather remote being, the least-called-upon superbeing among the major characters of the north Arabian pantheon.[^45] When there was drought, for example, the Safaitic-writing nomads appealed to Baʿal-Samīn, the storm god, directly, and not necessarily the creator god Allāh, to send the winds carrying rain.[^46] If he failed or was unresponsive, other divine powers could be enticed to fill his space. By far the most called upon superbeing is Allāt, a divinity associated with Venus.[^47] Invocations to her appeal for help in almost all matters of life. No pre-fourth century text presents the various members of the pantheon as intercessors meant to motivate a supreme god responsible for all wordly affairs to act. The ancient gods were not mediums but actors in their own right, and for this reason they populate the invocations of the Ancient North and South Arabian inscriptions.

The rise of Allāh as the sole deity invoked in the later Paleo-Arabic inscriptions appears to be an organic outgrowth of the monotheistic Zeitgeist acting upon a deity whose ancestral profile was ripe for such an evolution. Allāh’s identity as a creator god, attested in the Safaitic inscriptions predating significant biblical syncretism in the Hijaz, and the inherent parallels between his attributes, a light giving guidance, and the descriptions of Yahweh in the Hebrew Bible rendered him compatible with the supreme God of the monotheistic faiths.

Unlike in South Arabia, where older pagan gods were abandoned rather than reappropriated, the organic spread of this monotheistic framework throughout North Arabia, without an organized top-down intervention, led to a different kind of syncretism. The societal shifts spurred by the cultural and economic prestige of neighbouring monotheistic empires and communities would have led to a gradual redefinition of the cosmos. As Robin has suggested, the etymological and phonological similarities between allāh and the Christian *al-ʾilāh* would not have gone unappreciated.[^48] This linguistic and conceptual pathway facilitated the perception and eventual widespread equivalence of Allāh, the light-giving creator deity in ancestral Arabian religion, with the god of the Christians and the Jews. In this evolving moment, the autonomous agency of other traditional deities diminished. They were either reinterpreted as subordinate beings (angels or intercessors, as we shall discuss later in the Quranic context) or their public invocation became increasingly untenable, leading to the observed taboo against mentioning them in the public inscriptional medium. This process, by the sixth century, culminates in the Paleo-Arabic epigraphic record where Allāh stands alone, his name now resonating as the supreme, universal God, setting the stage for the theological declarations of the Quran. The diffusion of this recalibration of ancient Arabian religion is perhaps captured in real time in those proselytizing inscriptions, which call upon the Ḫazrag, a major tribe of Yathrib in the immediate pre-Islamic period, and others to show piety towards Allāh and to obey him.[^49]

## 3 | Reconciling the Data—The Epigraphy and the Quran

The preceding analyses have delineated two primary streams of evidence for the religious landscape of Late Antique Arabia: the evolving epigraphic record and the rich, albeit polemical testimony of the Quran. While much ink has been spilled on reconstructing the beliefs of the Quranic *mušrikīn* (lit. associators) or interpreting the inscriptions in isolation, both sources offer us different views of the same world. Yet, no systematic effort has been made until now to bring these two sources in conversation with one another. A principled synthesis is crucial for a more accurate and nuanced understanding of the milieu from which Islam emerged.

The epigraphic picture of an ascendant and exclusively invoked Allāh sets the stage for the understanding of the religious context of the Quran. However, the Quranic narrative, while affirming Allāh’s supreme status among the Prophet’s Meccan opponents, the *mušrikīn*, also details their veneration of other entities as intercessors (*šufaʿāʾ*), beings that the Quran sometimes labels as *ʾālihah* (deities/gods). Crone (2016) and Hawting (1999) have argued that these ancestral deities were reinterpreted in the synchronic religion of the *mušrikīn* as angelic figures, now subordinated within a broadly monotheistic framework. The use of *ʾilāh*, meaning ‘deity, god’, can easily be understood as a polemical device to underscore the undeserved veneration of beings other than Allāh. A similar usage is found in Q 5:116, which claims that Christians have taken Mary mother of Jesus as a god, *ʾilāh*. So then, while the Paleo-Arabic inscriptions document the disappearance of the ancient gods from the written record, the Quran, namely sūrah 53, reveals that at least three female entities, allāt, al-ʿuzzē, and manōh, names that correspond to pre-Islamic Arabian goddesses popular in the Ḥiǧāz,[^50] continued to be venerated in folk religion. The monotheistic Zeitgeist likely compelled a re-evaluation of the traditional divine hierarchy. The ancient gods, who once possessed autonomous power and received direct supplication for specific needs, as revealed in the Safaitic and Nabataean inscriptions,[^51] could no longer hold such status in a cosmos now dominated by a single, all-powerful creator. Their continued reverence, as Crone has argued, therefore depended on their reinterpretation as lesser, contingent beings (angels or intercessors) who did not rival Allāh but mediated access to him. That this was not simply a Meccan phenomenon is demonstrated by Sinai’s comprehensive study of the Ǧāhilī odes, where Allāh is virtually the only deity mentioned,[^52] and parallels the well-documented phenomenon of angel worship among late antique pagan monotheists.[^53] Such a theological demotion would explain their disappearance from the formal, public declarations of faith found in the Paleo-Arabic monotheistic inscriptions, which reflect the current of creator worship in Late Antiquity, while simultaneously accounting for their continued, albeit transformed, presence in the religious consciousness of the *mušrikīn* as described by the Quran. Moreover, this recalibration may account for the relative absence of distinct, individualized mythologies or clearly assigned cosmic responsibilities for these beings within the Quranic narrative itself. Whatever mythopoeia that might have existed in the more distant past, such as Allāt’s association with a celestial body (Venus) or Manōh with chance/fate, appear to have faded from active, dominant belief, or at least are not presented by the Quran as part of a living mythology among Mohammed’s contemporaries. The ‘goddesses’ survived as names that could be called upon for intercession and perhaps talismans that were presented during certain ceremonies, but the larger world to which they previously belonged had been completely obliterated.

The equation of ancient Allāh with the god of the Bible, and the subsequent reinterpretation of other superbeings as angels, is further supported by the role of the Meccan cult which, as argued by Goudarzi, became a crucial nexus for the articulation of this syncretic Arabian monotheism.[^54] The conflict with the Quraysh was not merely a political or tribal dispute but, in large measure, a struggle over the control and correct observance of rituals at the sacred site. Thus, Mohammed’s mission was not to introduce an entirely new god, à la *raḥmānān* in South Arabia, or to abolish an ancient sacred site, but rather, as S. Anthony has argued, to reform the existing Meccan cult.[^55] This reformation aimed to purge the sanctuary and its worship of practices deemed polytheistic (*širk*) such as specific types of sacrifices on cult stones or divinatory practices like casting lots with arrows, both of which are condemned in the Quran (Q 5:3). These were seen as elements distinguishing pagan worship from the ‘purified’ *dīn* of the Believers. The difference between Mohammed’s project in West Arabia, when compared to other monotheistic transformations that produced much more drastic breaks with the past, is comprehensible in light of the epigraphic record. Mohammed was not engaging with full-blown ancestral paganism but rather an Arabian belief system already significantly impacted by monotheism in an organic way. His mission was not the introduction of Allāh and his equation with the god of the Bible, but rather as Crone (2016) had insightfully argued, a reformation of what he considered to be an ‘imperfect’ monotheism. In practical terms, his project more thoroughly biblicized late antique West Arabian syncretic creator worship, drawing it closer to established monotheistic traditions like Judaism and Christianity. Since the Meccan sanctuary was already a site devoted to the worship of the creator, it did not need to be, or perhaps it could not have been, abandoned or destroyed, as was the fate of the pagan temples of South Arabia and elsewhere. Instead, Mohammed kept the pre-Islamic cult site intact but rather sought to reform the understanding of its associated rituals. The survival of ancestral Arabian *dīn*, according to Goudarzi’s understanding of this term, at the Meccan sanctuary again speaks to the organic diffusion of monotheistic trends in late antiquity—it was possible for an Arabian pilgrimage site to become devoted to the biblical god through the syncretism of creator deities, but without the intervention of organized religious institutions, ancestral rituals were simply put in biblical garb.[^56]

The organic, bottom-up spread of creator-centric worship to West Arabia helps address the complex issue of the *mušrikīn*’s reported beliefs concerning eschatology and divine forgiveness. One of the main points of tension between Mohammed and his opponents in Mecca was their denial of a bodily resurrection and consequently their failure to seek forgiveness (*istiġfār*) in an acceptable or efficacious manner. This seeming incompatibility with Judaism and Christianity at the time, both of which had an established belief in the bodily resurrection, is well accounted for in the present model. West Arabian monotheism, as expressed in the inscriptions, did not emerge from a conversion to any pre-existing faith system, but rather through the association of pre-existing compatibilities between ancestral Arabian religion and Judaism/Christianity. As such, while the notion of a creator God and the pious act of seeking his forgiveness for worldly transgressions might have gained traction (as both concepts previously existed),[^57] a corresponding belief in resurrection and detailed eschatological accountability were too foreign to diffuse in a casual, areal manner. But this, too, was not uniform as Crone does attempt to discern several groups in the Quran constituting the *mušrikīn*, only some of which denied the resurrection outright.[^58] The Quranic critique of this syncretism is laid bare in its admonition of pagans who do *istiġfār* ‘seeking forgiveness’. This prayer is attested in the Paleo-Arabic inscriptions, indicating that it was an invocational category before Mohammed’s ministry.[^59] Recognizing this, the Quranic intervention may be targeting this specific issue: the overlap of common ancestral and monotheistic practices in an attempt to redefine existing terminology, in the case of *ġfr*, to connect it with a belief in the hereafter, *al-ʾāḫirah*, and its eschatological underpinnings, rather than engaging in a full-scale replacement.

## 4 | But Where Have All the Idols Gone?

If the gradual diffusion of creator-centric worship as a part of the monotheistic Zeitgeist can reconcile the Quranic text with the inscriptions, there still remains the matter of later Muslim ‘memory’. The Hadith literature is rich with mentions of idols. Ibn al-Kalbī’s famous *Book of Idols* describes a pre-Islamic Arabia inhabited by worshippers of a diverse array of divine beings. Are these accounts to be understood as purely fictional in light of what we have discussed above? Some might find it hard to dismiss such a wide variety of sources. At the core of this tension we believe is an imprecise definition of what is meant by ‘god’ and perhaps an anachronistic and puritanical expectation of what a monotheist should believe and practice.

Less than a century ago, the nomads of north Arabia, who worshipped Allāh and identified as Muslims, would turn to another supernatural power in times of meteorological distress, ‘mother of rain’ (*umm al-ġayṯ*). During the 2024 Badia Epigraphic Survey campaign, Al-Jallad asked a local Bedouin sheikh of the Shurafāt tribe a generic question about strange customs no longer practiced. He said:

> *lēn mā tišti d-dinya yisawwūn ḫašabe yisawwūn zayy al-ādme yilabsūn hdūm il-ʾunṯa wa-yimšū fīhā wi-yġannūn maʿahā*
>
> When it wouldn’t rain, they would fashion a piece of wood in human form and dress it in female clothing and walk with it and sing with it.

The Sheikh informed us that the name of this idol was *umm al-ġayl*. The ritual that he recalled from local oral traditions corresponds quite closely to a custom that Musil described nearly 100 years earlier.

> If the earth does not receive abundant autumnal rain, the danger of a failure of the pasture, *maḥal*, arises. In order to prevent it, the girls and wives of the Bedouins form a procession with the *umm al-ġayṯ*, mother of the rain. A woman’s gown is stretched over two sticks forming a cross and is carried by a girl (virgin) at the head of the procession, which wanders from tent to tent singing:
>
> O mother of the rain! rain upon us;
> With pouring rain allay our thirst.
> O mother of the rain! rain upon us;
> From Allah’s measure, measure out to us.
> O mother of the rain! rain upon us;
> A real flood let our share be.
>
> Musil (1928: 10)[^60]

The Sheikh’s story is remarkable for demonstrating how memory of rituals can persist even after their practice has ceased, but also how the specific name of the obsolete idol can be altered in transmission. The more significant point, however, is that the belief in Allāh as the supreme creator does not preclude the existence of other supernatural entities that can be called upon to influence worldly affairs. Ultimately, the definition of ‘god’ is subjective and relative, and can be applied by outsiders polemically to any entity belonging to a tier of revered entities within a belief-specific framework. Thus, one man’s wooden intercessory effigy can be considered by someone on the outside as a ‘god,’ even if those engaged in such veneration would never apply such a label to the object of their devotion. Nevertheless, Umm al-Ġayṯ differs qualitatively from the ancient gods of indigenous Arabian religion. She has no independent personality, mythology, established relationship with other deities and supernatural forces, and a place in a pantheon. She is not conceptualized as a part of the divine cosmos. Rather, Umm al-Ġayṯ is simply an intercessory object, a superstitious device used to motivate rainfall. And as such, she is not incompatible with a monotheistic worldview. Songs and chants to her are simply modes of obtaining god’s favor; they are not considered worship, as such, by practitioners of this custom. Given this, perhaps it is wrong to even expect such an entity to show up epigraphically. Despite centuries of veneration, Umm al-Ġayṯ has not yet appeared in Islamic-period epigraphy. All known inscriptions call unequivocally upon Allāh, whether or not its inscribers invoked other beings on occasion.

The subjective line between ‘god’ and superstitious object has not been appreciated and this, we suspect, is largely what is responsible for the seeming incompatibility between ‘memories’ of the immediate pre-Islamic period and the material record. Ibn al-Kalbī’s material and idol-related traditions do not originate in the pre-Islamic period proper but reflect the antiquarian interests of the late eighth and ninth centuries. Writing in the early Abbasid period, over a century after the rise of Islam, Ibn al-Kalbī was compiling a collection of fragmented oral traditions, etymological explanations for names, and folkloric accounts of ritual practices. It is plausible that many of these pertained to what might be better understood as local numina, superstitious objects, features of the landscape or eponymous ancestral figures, rather than a coherent, pan-Arabian pantheon of superbeings retaining active and widespread cults. In fact, a close examination of ibn al-Kalbī’s list of ‘idols’ reveals just this. The only names that find correlates in ancient Arabian religion are those mentioned in the Quran: *allāt*, *al-ʿuzzē*, and *manōh*, which are mentioned in Sūrah 53 and were likewise worshipped widely in pre-fourth century West Arabia, and the five Noahide ‘gods’ (*ālihah*) *nasr*, *suwāʿ*, *yaġūṯ*, *yaʿūq*, and *wadd* mentioned in the Q 71:23.[^61] With a couple of exceptions (see below), the remaining of ibn al-Kalbī’s idols are nowhere to be found epigraphically.[^62] The obvious explanation for this is that the Quran is addressing re-interpreted divine beings, in the case of allāt, al-ʿuzzē and manōh, and using a genuine memory of ancient, no-longer-worshipped deities in Q 71. The placement of *nasr*, *suwāʿ*, *yaġūṯ*, *yaʿūq* and *wadd* in antediluvian times underscores the remoteness the audience felt with regard to these names; they could not have been actively worshipped at the time of the audience if there were to be understood as being a part of the idolatry of the world God wished to cleanse with the deluge. So, while the Quran is specifically addressing mythological superbeings of ancient Arabian religion, ibn al-Kalbī’s other idols belong to a different category. They are drawn from the stories of locals, similar to the conversation Al-Jallad has with the Sheikh of the Shurafāt in the Ḥarrah. The complete absence of these figures in the epigraphic record, in contrast to the Quranic gods, is best explained by appreciating their different nature. The tribal idols like *ḏū l-ḫalaṣah*, *saʿd*, *nuhm*, and others serve in ibn al-Kalbī’s narrative to underscore the virtues of monotheism and the impotence of superstitious objects. To illustrate:

> Malik and the Milkan, the two sons of Kinanah, had at the coast of Juddah [or somewhere] in that region, an idol called Saʿd. It was a long rock. Once upon a time a certain man [belonging to one of these tribes] came to it with his flock of camels in order to make them stand on it and thereby obtain its blessing. But as he led them near the rock they shied away from it because it was covered with blood, and they scattered in every direction. Thereupon the man became furious, and picked up a stone and threw it at the rock saying, “Accursed god! Thou hast caused my camels to shy.” He then went after them until he gathered them, and returned home saying: We came to Saʿd in hope he would unite our ranks, But he broke them up. We will have none of him. Is he not but a rock in a barren land, Deaf to both evil and to good?
>
> (Trans. Faris 1952)

It is likely that these other figures were never members of an ancient Arabian pantheon. Indeed, nothing in ibn al-Kalbī’s book suggests the existence of a coherent mythological framework into which these idols fit. No stories detail their character or legendary roles in the cosmos. They are instead presented as venerated objects tied to particular localities, tribes or needs, existing within a broader worldview where Allāh was already widely acknowledged as the supreme deity. It is in fact ibn al-Kalbī’s theoretical thinking that has collapsed this category of superstition with the ancient superbeings of pre-Islamic Arabian religion(s), labelling them all ‘gods’. Once we recognize this difference, the seeming incompatibility between the ‘gods’ populating the Ǧāhiliyyah of Islamic literary sources and the ‘monotheism’ of the Paleo-Arabic inscriptions evaporates.

While Crone is probably right that the Meccan audience of Mohammed understood the goddesses of Sūrah 53 as angels, it is remarkable that there is no trace of this notion in the *Book of Idols*. The explanation for this, too, seems simple. By ibn al-Kalbī’s time, memory of authentic, late antique Arabian syncretic belief (*širk*) had been all but forgotten in the wake of the total Islamicization of Mecca. As such, there was no community to preserve ancient theological understandings, nor was there any practical purpose to do so. While ibn al-Kalbī had access to the names of genuine pre-Islamic Arabian gods from the Quran, his only way to understand them was through the ‘memories’ of his contemporaries, which reflected the practice of venerating superstitious objects and talismans and not the cults of the ancient gods, by that time long forgotten. Thus, Allāt and her sisters were fit into the model of *umm al-ġayṯ*. They became featureless, stereotyped superstitious objects: rocks, trees, and so forth. With their original mythologies forgotten, they took on new roles in the story of the rise of Islam, acting as antagonists to face off against Mohammed’s generals.[^63] Their destruction served a literary function, symbolizing the end of idolatry in Arabia.

## 5 | Conclusions

Reading the epigraphy and the Quran in conjunction, with due attention to the political and social currents of the late antique world, paints a picture of sixth century West Arabia as a region undergoing profound religious transformations. The ancestral figure of Allāh, the ancient creator deity of the North Arabian pantheon, was increasingly identified with the supreme, universal God, reflecting the monotheistic Zeitgeist of late antiquity. He became the exclusive object of public invocation throughout the Ḥiǧāz in the century prior to Mohammed’s ministry. This transformation, however, was not monolithic. It allowed for the persistence of reinterpreted supernatural intermediaries in the worldview of some, like the Quranic *mušrikīn*, and a varied engagement with the theological corollaries of monotheism, such as divine forgiveness and the afterlife. This recalibration explains why these superbeings appear in pre-Islamic West Arabian cultic practice, as explicated in the Quran, but are absent from the inscriptional record, in sharp contrast to the inscriptions from earlier centuries. The proliferation of creator-worship led to the demotion and eventual extinction of the ancient gods, but other folk practices, such as superstitious objects, talismans, and landmarks persisted as they did not conceptually contradict the belief in a single, all-powerful ‘god’.[^64] Indeed, it was the conflation of ‘superbeing’ and ‘superstitious object’, under the exchangeable labels of *ʾilāh*, *ṣanam* and *waṯan*, by writers in the early Islamic period, and their modern readers, that created a literary Ǧāhiliyyah seemingly at odds with the Quran and material record. The sixth century Meccan cult, in this light, can then be understood as a distinct expression of Arabian syncretic creator worship, blending remnants of ancestral Arabian religion with the established monotheistic traditions surrounding it. This was the world Mohammed was born into and the one his movement sought to reform.
