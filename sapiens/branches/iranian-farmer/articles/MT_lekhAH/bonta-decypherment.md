## Uniqueness
The American linguist/ethnographer Steven Bonta has proposed an Indo-Aryan, i.e., Sanskrit decipherment of the Harappan script. His approach is quite different from many of the previous attempts, like Mitchiner, S.R. Rao, ironically part of Parpola's thesis ("dAsa Aryans"; his Dravidianist interpretation notwithstanding), Jha-Rajaram, and Yaj~nadev on this medium. 

## Pros
While today, there are serious difficulties for an Indo-Aryan interpretation of Harappan, there are aspects of Bonta's hypothesis that are well-argued:

1. His "positional structuralism" is a good approach that has been ignored by many of the previous code-breakers.
2. His pushback against the "not a script" hypothesis of Farmer et al has convinced me that this was indeed a kind of script with real linguistic content.
3. In this regard, he is probably right in seeing in it a logo-syllabic system.
4. He argues that the presence of compounds (samAsa-s), including AmreDita-s, is a key feature distinguishing its typology from Dravidian. 
   
The Dravidian hypothesis was always problematic for anyone who closely studied the problem of the substrate in Indo-Aryan. One can also argue against it being a "para-Munda" language, contra Witzel. Hence, I think he is capturing some real features of the Harappan language.

## Cons
However, apart from his Sanskrit readings, there are some problems with his interpretations that may or may not be insurmountable:

1. Like some of the interpretations before his, his too suffers from the vowel issue: long-short, o-u etc. Vowels are important in a language like Sanskrit. However, his proposal is not too far-fetched.
2. A metrological function for the seal is strange. Why hard-code numerals into a baked seal? After all, quantities are likely to change, even if they were weights.
3. He uses "mIn" for the famous fish sign – something shared with the Dravidologists. They would shoot back – hey, that means it was a proto-Dravidian which had features different from the much later attested Dravidian, like the earliest Tamil.

Yet it is remarkable that he reached an Indo-Aryan endpoint given that a guy with his background would have gravitated towards Dravidian. I think, in that alone there is utility - for it suggests that when the evidence is objectively examined the less likely Dravidian seems.
