Walk

Charles Dickens fought his depression by walking through London at night. One October he set out at 2 in the morning and walked 30 miles, all the way to his country home in Kent. In 1860 he wrote about why it worked. It took psychology another 150 years to catch up.

Dickens called his bad spells “spectres.” They came back every time he started a new novel and sometimes hung on for months. His mood would fall apart, his sleep would collapse, and the only thing that pulled him out was walking.

He explained his method in an essay called “Night Walks,” published on July 21, 1860 in his weekly magazine All the Year Round. He had tried fighting his insomnia from bed and lost. So he changed the plan. The fix, he wrote, was “getting up directly after lying down, and going out, and coming home tired at sunrise.” A worried mind cannot fix itself by worrying more in bed. You have to get up and move.

Most nights he walked 12 to 20 miles. A friend called it “violent walking.” Dickens wrote that on these walks his wandering self had “many miles upon miles of streets in which it could, and did, have its own solitary way.”

Today, walking is one of the most powerful tools doctors have against depression. In 2012 a team of researchers pulled together eight high-quality studies of walking as a depression treatment. The effect was as strong as the antidepressants doctors actually prescribe.

The biggest test came from Duke University. The SMILE study took 202 adults with serious depression and split them into four groups: supervised exercise, home exercise, the drug Zoloft, or a placebo pill. After 16 weeks, the people who exercised did just as well as the people on Zoloft. A 2024 review of 75 studies covering 8,636 patients confirmed it. Walking should be one of the first things doctors try.

The reason is the thing Dickens stumbled onto in the dark. Depression runs on rumination, the looping bad thoughts that grind people down during the worst stretches. In 2015 Stanford researchers scanned people’s brains before and after a 90-minute walk in a quiet park. The walkers had less activity in a part of the brain called the subgenual prefrontal cortex. That spot, deep behind your forehead, is the brain’s worry loop. After the walk, the worry loop got quieter. The walkers said they felt less stuck inside their own heads. The brain scans agreed.

A walking body shuts up a noisy mind. The street takes attention, the walking rhythm fills the head, and the dark spells lose their grip. Dickens called the streets his cure because they gave his brain somewhere else to be. The science 150 years later says he had it right. Depression hates a brain that is moving.

Shower

Part 2. Charles Dickens swam in freezing sea water every morning before he could write. He built himself an ice-cold shower under a 150-foot waterfall in 1849. He wrote letters explaining why it worked. The chemistry of what he was doing has only just been measured.

Dickens called it the discipline of “air, exercise and cold water.” He drank cold water night and morning. He also poured it down his back. He swam in the Thames at sunrise from Petersham to Richmond Bridge. He swam in the icy English Channel at Broadstairs, in the cold sea off the Isle of Wight, and in the Mediterranean off the coast of Italy. In 1851 he took his wife to Malvern for hydrotherapy.

In 1849, while staying at a house in Bonchurch on the Isle of Wight, Dickens noticed a waterfall on the property cascading 150 feet down a cliff. He hired a carpenter to route the water into an outdoor shower bath. Every morning he would first swim in the cold sea, then stand under the freezing waterfall, then climb to the highest point on the island. Only then did he sit down to write.

When you plunge into water at around 14°C, the temperature of the English Channel for most of the year, your body fires what scientists call the cold shock response. Blood vessels clamp shut and your heart races. You gasp for breath. Then within seconds, two chemicals flood your bloodstream.

In 2000 a physiology study measured both chemicals in young men submerged in 14°C water. Norepinephrine jumped by 530%. Dopamine jumped by 250%. The dopamine boost lasted 2 to 3 hours after they got out of the water. Norepinephrine is the chemical antidepressants target. Dopamine handles motivation, focus, and reward.

A 2023 brain imaging study scanned 33 adults before and after 5 minutes in cool water. The cold reshaped how major brain networks talked to each other. Participants reported feeling sharper and more inspired afterward, and their positive-to-negative emotion ratio nearly doubled.

A 2025 meta-analysis pooled 11 randomized trials and found cold water immersion improves sleep quality and quality of life. Mood effects were mixed, though the trend leaned toward better mood. The science is still being worked out.

Wim Hof has built a global wellness empire on roughly the same biology. Cold plunge tubs are now standard equipment in gyms, wellness retreats, and tech offices.

Dickens did this every morning of his adult life. The Bonchurch waterfall he built still flows. He could not write a single word until he had been in cold water first. The chemicals have names now. He just called it hydrotherapy.

Embodied cognition

Part 3. Charles Dickens wrote his greatest characters by acting them out in a mirror. He would jump from his desk, contort his face, speak in their voices, then run back to write. His daughter watched from a sofa. Modern science calls this embodied cognition.

In 1885, his daughter Mamie published a memoir of her father. She described one morning at their London home of Tavistock House. She was recovering from a long illness, and her father had let her rest in his study. He wrote furiously at his desk. Then he jumped up, ran to a nearby mirror, and started making what she called “extraordinary facial contortions.”

He ran back to the desk and wrote furiously for a few minutes, then went back to the mirror. The pantomime resumed. Then he started talking aloud, quickly, in a low voice. He turned toward Mamie, but he was not seeing her. He had become the character he was writing.

He did this for characters across all his novels. His other daughter Kate said the same. In his garden chalet at Gad’s Hill, mirrors lined the walls so he could see himself from every angle while writing. He created nearly 1,000 distinct characters across his career. He built most of them in his own face first.

Modern psychology has a name for what he was doing: embodied cognition. The body and the brain work together as partners in thought. Facial expressions, gestures, and postures feed information back to the brain and shape what it can imagine, feel, and produce.

Make your face smile and your brain reports more positive emotion. Frown and the report flips. The classic study from 1988 used a pen. People holding a pen between their teeth, which forces a smile, rated cartoons funnier than people holding it between their lips, which forces a frown. Same cartoons. Different felt emotions. Decades of follow-up research support the basic principle.

A 2012 brain imaging study scanned professional actors performing intense scenes. The actors showed measurable down-regulation of the amygdala, the brain’s threat detection center, while expressing anger and fear. Their bodies were managing the emotion in real time.

A 2026 systematic review of acting research found that physically embodying characters improves emotion recognition, empathy, theory of mind, and perspective-taking. Acting training gives you a kind of emotional dial. You can feel a strong emotion on cue, then turn it back down when the scene ends.

Dickens did not know any of this. He just figured out, by instinct and by mirror, that he could not write a character he had not first lived in his own face. The face came first. The page came second.
