There can be personal reasons for a genius to claim he's not really special: perhaps sometimes ideological reasons, but more often  trying to avoid interpersonal friction. How do we clarify this kind of  camouflage? We might ask their peers..

for example, what do others -   colleagues, think of Terry Tao?
John Garnett, former UCLA math chair, said when Tao won the Fields Medal: “Terry is like Mozart; mathematics just flows out of him,” adding that mathematicians with Tao’s talent appear “only once in a generation” and that he was “probably the best mathematician in the world right now.”

Richard Feynman would say he was not a special person: but what did his peers say?
Hans Bethe: Feynman was a “magician.” Bethe’s contrast was that ordinary geniuses do great things in ways you can imagine copying if you were much better; with magicians, “you can have no idea how they do it.” His verdict: “Feynman was a magician.”

John Von Neumman never tried this " jist folks" schtick.  But what did his peers say?  
Hans Bethe' "I have sometimes wondered whether a brain like von Neumann's does not indicate a species superior to that of man."  
George Pólya, his old teacher at ETH: "Johnny was the only student I was ever afraid of."
