Blots on a field?

A neuroscience image sleuth finds signs of fabrication in scores of Alzheimer‚Äôs articles, threatening a reigning theory of the disease

Neuroscientist and physician Matthew Schrag found suspect images in dozens of papers involving Alzheimer‚Äôs disease, including Western blots (projected in green) measuring a protein linked to cognitive decline in rats.Joseph Ross

A version of this story appeared in Science, Vol 377, Issue 6604.

In August 2021, Matthew Schrag, a neuroscientist and physician at Vanderbilt University, got a call that would plunge him into a maelstrom of possible scientific misconduct. A colleague wanted to connect him with an attorney investigating an experimental drug for Alzheimer‚Äôs disease called Simufilam. The drug‚Äôs developer, Cassava Sciences, claimed it improved cognition, partly by repairing a protein that can block sticky brain deposits of the protein amyloid beta (AŒ≤), a hallmark of Alzheimer‚Äôs. The attorney‚Äôs clients‚Äîtwo prominent neuroscientists who are also short sellers who profit if the company‚Äôs stock falls‚Äîbelieved some research related to Simufilam may have been ‚Äúfraudulent,‚Äù according to a petition later filed on their behalf with the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA).

Schrag, 37, a softspoken, nonchalantly rumpled junior professor, had already gained some notoriety by publicly criticizing the controversial FDA approval of the anti-AŒ≤ drug Aduhelm. His own research also contradicted some of Cassava‚Äôs claims. He feared volunteers in ongoing Simufilam trials faced risks of side effects with no chance of benefit.

So he applied his technical and medical knowledge to interrogate published images about the drug and its underlying science‚Äîfor which the attorney paid him $18,000. He identified apparently altered or duplicated images in dozens of journal articles. The attorney reported many of the discoveries in the FDA petition, and Schrag sent all of them to the National Institutes of Health (NIH), which had invested tens of millions of dollars in the work. (Cassava denies any misconduct [see sidebar, below].)

But Schrag‚Äôs sleuthing drew him into a different episode of possible misconduct, leading to findings that threaten one of the most cited Alzheimer‚Äôs studies of this century and numerous related experiments.

The first author of that influential study, published in Nature in 2006, was an ascending neuroscientist: Sylvain Lesn√© of the University of Minnesota (UMN), Twin Cities. His work underpins a key element of the dominant yet controversial amyloid hypothesis of Alzheimer‚Äôs, which holds that AŒ≤ clumps, known as plaques, in brain tissue are a primary cause of the devastating illness, which afflicts tens of millions globally. In what looked like a smoking gun for the theory and a lead to possible therapies, Lesn√© and his colleagues discovered an AŒ≤ subtype and seemed to prove it caused dementia in rats. If Schrag‚Äôs doubts are correct, Lesn√©‚Äôs findings were an elaborate mirage.

Schrag, who had not publicly revealed his role as a whistleblower until this article, avoids the word ‚Äúfraud‚Äù in his critiques of Lesn√©‚Äôs work and the Cassava-related studies and does not claim to have proved misconduct. That would require access to original, complete, unpublished images and in some cases raw numerical data. ‚ÄúI focus on what we can see in the published images, and describe them as red flags, not final conclusions,‚Äù he says. ‚ÄúThe data should speak for itself.‚Äù Advertisement

A 6-month investigation by Science provided strong support for Schrag‚Äôs suspicions and raised questions about Lesn√©‚Äôs research. A leading independent image analyst and several top Alzheimer‚Äôs researchers‚Äîincluding George Perry of the University of Texas, San Antonio, and John Forsayeth of the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF)‚Äîreviewed most of Schrag‚Äôs findings at Science‚Äôs request. Some look like ‚Äúshockingly blatant‚Äù examples of image tampering, says Donna Wilcock, an Alzheimer‚Äôs expert at the University of Kentucky.

The authors ‚Äúappeared to have composed figures by piecing together parts of photos from different experiments,‚Äù says Elisabeth Bik, a molecular biologist and well-known forensic image consultant. ‚ÄúThe obtained experimental results might not have been the desired results, and that data might have been changed to ‚Ä¶ better fit a hypothesis.‚Äù quotation mark The immediate, obvious damage is wasted NIH funding and wasted thinking in the field because people are using these results as a starting point for their own experiments.

Early this year, Schrag raised his doubts with NIH and journals including Nature; two, including Nature last week, have published expressions of concern about papers by Lesn√©. Schrag‚Äôs work, done independently of Vanderbilt and its medical center, implies millions of federal dollars may have been misspent on the research‚Äîand much more on related efforts. Some Alzheimer‚Äôs experts now suspect Lesn√©‚Äôs studies have misdirected Alzheimer‚Äôs research for 16 years.

‚ÄúThe immediate, obvious damage is wasted NIH funding and wasted thinking in the field because people are using these results as a starting point for their own experiments,‚Äù says Stanford University neuroscientist Thomas S√ºdhof, a Nobel laureate and expert on Alzheimer‚Äôs and related conditions.

Lesn√© did not respond to requests for comment. A UMN spokesperson says the university is reviewing complaints about his work.

To Schrag, the two disputed threads of AŒ≤ research raise far-reaching questions about scientific integrity in the struggle to understand and cure Alzheimer‚Äôs. Some adherents of the amyloid hypothesis are too uncritical of work that seems to support it, he says. ‚ÄúEven if misconduct is rare, false ideas inserted into key nodes in our body of scientific knowledge can warp our understanding.‚Äù

In his modest office, steps away from a buzzing refrigerator, Schrag displays an antique microscope‚Äîan homage to predecessors who applied painstaking bench science to medicine‚Äôs endless enigmas. A small sign on his desk reads, ‚ÄúEverything is figureoutable.‚Äù

So far, Alzheimer‚Äôs has been an exception. But Schrag‚Äôs background has left him comfortable with the field‚Äôs contradictions. His father hails from a family of Mennonites, known for their philosophy of peacemaking‚Äîbut joined the military. The family moved from Arizona to Germany to England before settling in Davenport, a tiny cow town in eastern Washington. After leaving the Air Force, Schrag‚Äôs dad became a nurse and worked in a nursing home. As a young teen, Schrag volunteered to visit dementia patients there. ‚ÄúI remembered being mystified by a lot of the strange behaviors,‚Äù he says. It was a formative experience ‚Äúto see people struggling with such unfair symptoms.‚Äù

Home-schooled by his mom, Schrag entered community college at 16, like many of the town‚Äôs studious kids‚Äîincluding his teenage sweetheart and future wife, Sarah. They now live on a small ranch outside Nashville with their two young children and three aging horses that Sarah grew up with.

While prepping for medical school at the University of North Dakota, Schrag spent long hours in a neuropharmacology lab absorbing the patient rhythms of science. He repeated experiments over and over, refining his skills. These included a protein identification method known as the Western blot. It uses electricity to drive protein-rich tissue samples through a gel that acts like a sieve to separate the molecules by size. Distinct proteins, tagged and illuminated by fluorescent antibodies, appear as stacked bands.

In 2006, Schrag‚Äôs first publication examined how feeding a high-cholesterol diet to rabbits seemed to increase AŒ≤ plaques and iron deposits in one part of their brains. Not long afterward, when he was an M.D.-Ph.D. student at Loma Linda University, another research group found support for a link between Alzheimer‚Äôs and iron metabolism. Encouraged, Schrag poured his energy into trying to confirm the connection in people‚Äîand failed. The experience introduced him to a disquieting element of Alzheimer‚Äôs research. With this enigmatic, complex disease, even careful experiments done in good faith can fail to replicate, leading to dead ends and unexpected setbacks.

One of its biggest mysteries is also its most distinctive feature: the plaques and other protein deposits that German pathologist Alois Alzheimer first saw in 1906 in the brain of a deceased dementia patient. In 1984, AŒ≤ was identified as the main component of the plaques. And in 1991, researchers traced family-linked Alzheimer‚Äôs to mutations in the gene for a precursor protein from which amyloid derives. To many scientists, it seemed clear that AŒ≤ buildup sets off a cascade of damage and dysfunction in neurons, causing dementia. Stopping amyloid deposits became the most plausible therapeutic strategy.

Hundreds of clinical trials of amyloidtargeted therapies have yielded few glimmers of promise, however; only the underwhelming Aduhelm has gained FDA approval. Yet AŒ≤ still dominates research and drug development. NIH spent about $1.6 billion on projects that mention amyloids in this fiscal year, about half its overall Alzheimer‚Äôs funding. Scientists who advance other potential Alzheimer‚Äôs causes, such as immune dysfunction or inflammation, complain they have been sidelined by the ‚Äúamyloid mafia.‚Äù Forsayeth says the amyloid hypothesis became ‚Äúthe scientific equivalent of the Ptolemaic model of the Solar System,‚Äù in which the Sun and planets rotate around Earth.

By 2006, the centenary of Alois Alzheimer‚Äôs epic discovery, a growing cadre of skeptics wondered aloud whether the field needed a reset. Then, a breathtaking Nature paper entered the breach.

It emerged from the lab of UMN physician and neuroscientist Karen Ashe, who had already made a remarkable series of discoveries. As a medical resident at UCSF, she contributed to Nobel laureate Stanley Prusiner‚Äôs pioneering work on prions‚Äîinfectious proteins that cause rare neurological disorders. In the mid-1990s, she created a transgenic mouse that churns out human AŒ≤, which forms plaques in the animal‚Äôs brain. The mouse also shows dementia-like symptoms. It became a favored Alzheimer‚Äôs model.

By the early 2000s, ‚Äútoxic oligomers,‚Äù subtypes of AŒ≤ that dissolve in some bodily fluids, had gained currency as a likely chief culprit for Alzheimer‚Äôs‚Äîpotentially more pathogenic than the insoluble plaques. Amyloid oligomers had been linked to impaired communication between neurons in vitro and in animals, and autopsies have shown higher levels of the oligomers in people with Alzheimer‚Äôs than in cognitively sound individuals. But no one had proved that any one of the many known oligomers directly caused cognitive decline. Sylvain Lesn√©University of Minnesota, Twin Cities N. Desai/Science

In the brains of Ashe‚Äôs transgenic mice, the UMN team discovered a previously unknown oligomer species, dubbed AŒ≤56 (pronounced ‚Äúamyloid beta star 56‚Äù) after its relatively heavy molecular weight compared with other oligomers. The group isolated AŒ≤56 and injected it into young rats. The rats‚Äô capacity to recall simple, previously learned information‚Äîsuch as the location of a hidden platform in a maze‚Äîplummeted. The 2006 paper‚Äôs first author, sometimes credited as the discoverer of AŒ≤*56, was Lesn√©, a young scientist Ashe had hired straight out of a Ph.D. program at the University of Caen Normandy in France.

Ashe touted AŒ≤56 on her website as ‚Äúthe first substance ever identified in brain tissue in Alzheimer‚Äôs research that has been shown to cause memory impairment.‚Äù An accompanying editorial in Nature called AŒ≤56 ‚Äúa star suspect‚Äù in Alzheimer‚Äôs. The Nature paper has been cited in about 2300 scholarly articles‚Äîmore than all but four other Alzheimer‚Äôs basic research reports published since 2006, according to the Web of Science database. Since then, annual NIH support for studies labeled ‚Äúamyloid, oligomer, and Alzheimer‚Äôs‚Äù has risen from near zero to $287 million in 2021. The paper provided an ‚Äúimportant boost‚Äù to the amyloid and toxic oligomer hypotheses when they faced rising doubts, S√ºdhof says. ‚ÄúProponents loved it, because it seemed to be an independent validation of what they have been proposing for a long time.‚Äù

‚ÄúThat was a really big finding that kind of turned the field on its head,‚Äù partly because of Ashe‚Äôs impeccable imprimatur, Wilcock says. ‚ÄúIt drove a lot of other investigators to ‚Ä¶ go looking for these [heavier] oligomer species.‚Äù

As Ashe‚Äôs star burned more brightly, Lesn√©‚Äôs rose. He joined UMN with his own NIH-funded lab in 2009. AŒ≤*56 remained a primary research focus. He became a leader of UMN‚Äôs neuroscience graduate program in 2020, and in May 2022, 4 months after Schrag delivered his concerns to NIH, Lesn√© received a coveted R01 grant from the agency, with up to 5 years of support. In December 2021, Schrag visited PubPeer, a website where scientists flag possible errors in published papers. Such manipulations can falsely suggest a protein is present‚Äîor alter the levels at which a detected protein is apparently found. Schrag, still focused on Cassava-linked scientists, was looking for examples that could refine his own sleuthing.

In a PubPeer search for ‚ÄúAlzheimer‚Äôs,‚Äù postings about articles in The Journal of Neuroscience caught Schrag‚Äôs eye. Three of the papers listed Lesn√©, whom Schrag had never heard of, as first or senior author. Schrag quickly found that another Lesn√© paper had also drawn scrutiny on PubPeer, and he broadened his search to Lesn√© papers that had not been flagged there. ‚ÄúSo much in our field is not reproducible, so it‚Äôs a huge advantage to understand when data streams might not be reliable,‚Äù Schrag says. ‚ÄúSome of that‚Äôs going to happen reproducing data on the bench. In the 16 years following the landmark paper, Lesn√© and Ashe‚Äîseparately or jointly‚Äîpublished many articles on their stellar oligomer. Yet only a handful of other groups have reported detecting AŒ≤*56.

Citing the ongoing UMN review of Lesn√©‚Äôs work, Ashe declined via email to be interviewed or to answer written questions posed by Science, which she called ‚Äúsobering.‚Äù But she wrote, ‚ÄúI still have faith in AŒ≤*56,‚Äù noting her ongoing work studying the structure of AŒ≤ oligomers. ‚ÄúWe have promising initial results. But even before Schrag‚Äôs investigation, the spotty evidence that AŒ≤56 plays a role in Alzheimer‚Äôs had raised eyebrows. Wilcock has long doubted studies that claim to use ‚Äúpurified‚Äù AŒ≤56. In fact, Wilcock and others say, several labs have tried and failed to find AŒ≤56, although few have published those findings. Journals are often uninterested in negative results, and researchers can be reluctant to contradict a famous investigator.

An exception was Harvard University‚Äôs Dennis Selkoe, a leading advocate of the amyloid and toxic oligomer hypotheses, who has cited the Nature paper at least 13 times. In two 2008 papers, Selkoe said he could not find AŒ≤*56 in human fluids or tissues.
