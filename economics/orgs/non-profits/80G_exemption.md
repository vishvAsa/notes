## Common Reasons for 80G Refusal
### For Vedapatasalas/Religious Trusts

For a trust engaged in Vedic education or temple-associated activities (*Religious cum Charitable*), the Income Tax Department frequently raises the following objections to reject 80G applications:

*   **The "Wholly Religious" Classification:**
    Under Indian tax law, purely religious trusts are **not eligible** for 80G registration. Only "charitable" or "charitable-cum-religious" trusts are eligible. If the Commissioner of Income Tax (Exemptions) [CIT(E)] views the teaching of Vedas and temple *kainkaryam* as purely religious rituals rather than educational or cultural preservation, they may reject the 80G application.
*   **Exceeding the 5% Religious Expenditure Limit (Section 80G(5B)):**
    By law, a composite (charitable-cum-religious) trust can spend money on religious activities, but **this expenditure must not exceed 5% of its total income** in that financial year. If the trust's accounts show that a major portion of funds is spent on temple recitations, rituals, or daily worship, the CIT(E) will reject the 80G on the grounds of violating this 5% limit.
*   **Benefiting a Specific Caste or Community:**
    Section 80G(5)(iii) states that the trust’s activities must not be restricted to any particular religious community or caste. If the CIT(E) interprets the *Vedapatasala* as serving or admitting only a specific community, it can trigger a rejection.
*   **Technical or Procedural Rejections:**
    Under the updated rules, if a trust files the wrong form (e.g., choosing the wrong clause in Form 10A/10AB) or fails to submit audited accounts and registration deeds on time, the application is rejected on hyper-technical grounds.

## Can an 80G Refusal Be Appealed?
**Yes, absolutely.** If the CIT(Exemptions) rejects an 80G application, the trust has a clear legal remedy to appeal the decision.

*   **Where to file:** The appeal must be filed before the **Income Tax Appellate Tribunal (ITAT)** under whose jurisdiction the trust falls.
*   **Timeline:** The appeal must be filed within **60 days** from the date on which the rejection order is received by the trust.
*   **Form used:** The appeal is filed using **Form No. 36**.

### Vedic organization appeal

If you appeal an 80G rejection before the ITAT, your Chartered Accountant or legal counsel can rely on several strong, established legal precedents to secure approval:

#### Argument A: Vedic Education is "Education" (Charitable), Not "Religion"
Under Section 2(15) of the Income Tax Act, "Education" is a recognized charitable purpose.
*   **The Defense:** Imparting the study of Vedas, Sanskrit, and ancient heritage to young students in a structured environment (a *Pathasala*) constitutes "education" and the "preservation of monument/cultural heritage."
*   Several ITAT benches have ruled that running a school to teach traditional scriptures is an educational activity for the preservation of culture, which qualifies as a charitable activity rather than a dogmatic religious ritual.

#### Argument B: The 5% Limit Applies to Actual Spending, Not the Trust's Objects
If the CIT(E) rejects the application simply because the trust's deed mentions religious terms (like *kainkaryam* or *temple services*):
*   **The Defense:** Higher courts and the ITAT have consistently ruled that **merely having a religious object in a trust deed does not bar 80G approval.**
*   The CIT(E) must look at the *actual expenditure*. If the actual money spent on purely religious rituals (like pujas or samagri) is less than 5% of the total income, the trust is legally entitled to 80G approval under Section 80G(5B).

#### Argument C: Promotion of Moral and Spiritual Values is Secular
*   **The Defense:** Organising spiritual lectures, teaching moral values, and reciting texts for the general public's peace and well-being are considered spiritual upliftment for humanity, which is secular and charitable, not restricted to any one community.
