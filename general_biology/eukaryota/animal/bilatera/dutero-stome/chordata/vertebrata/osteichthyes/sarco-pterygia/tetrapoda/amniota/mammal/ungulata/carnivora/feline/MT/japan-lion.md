Did Japan have tigers or lions?

Fossils of a big cat had been found in Japan, and people assumed they were the now extinct Japanese tiger. However, Chinese workers recently sequenced the mitogenomes and partial proteomic fragments of 5 Japanese "tigers," and it turned out they were lions, not tigers. 

The aDNA investigations and other investigations reveal that there were 5 types of lions: 1. The basal-most maned Panthera leo; 2. The American lion Panthera atrox; 3. The European lion, Panthera fossilis; 4. Type-I cave lion, Panthera spelaea-1; 5. Type-II cave lion, Panthera spelaea-2. The cave lions lacked manes.

The authors show that while the tiger reached North East Asia/Siberia, it did not cross over to the extreme northeast into Beringia and America. However, three waves of lions, starting with P.atrox and the two P.spelaea types, made it to the extreme NE and then into America. Of these, P. atrox spread all through North America, reaching up to southern Mexico, while P.spelaea types remained confined to Alaska. The Japanese lions were part of the first wave of cave lions, Panthera spelaea-1, that remained in Japan till around 18K years ago. Thus, the Japanese lion survived the destructive effects of the arrival of Homo (the ancestors of the Jomon) for a long time (~20-22K years). Thus, the lion radiation pushed into places where the tiger and the leopard never reached.
