Wonder how the angiosperms – the most common plants we see around us today – are related to each other and when they arose? 

## 2026 study
Source: [TW](https://nature.com/articles/s41477-026-02311-x)
A recent study suggests that:

1. The common ancestor of all extant angiosperms emerged in the late Jurassic, a little before 150 million years ago.

2. The angiosperms underwent an explosive radiation between 130-110 Mya in the Cretaceous, giving rise to all the major modern lineages of flowering plants.

3. The three basal lineages of angiosperms, Amborellales, Nymphaeales, and Austrobaileyales, are the leftovers from their earliest radiation in the closing stages of the Jurassic. For a layperson, the most likely plants from this early radiation that one might encounter are the water lilies and the star anise.

4. The radiation of the ornithischian dinosaurs in the Cretaceous seems to have gone hand in hand with the explosive radiation of the angiosperms. Which drove which is hard to disentangle, but there was possibly a feedback loop with these low-browsing dinosaurs favoring the spread of angiosperms in ecosystems and vice versa.
