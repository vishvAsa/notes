## Newton
Newton publicly said regarding the action of gravity at a distance through a vacuum: "Hypotheses non fingo": I frame no hypotheses. However, he had a clear-cut belief in this regard. He viewed the vacuum of space not as empty nothingness, but as the sensorium Dei – the sense field of his god. Thus, gravity was the direct result of this god actively sustaining the motion of the universe (A view Leibniz ridiculed). 

## Einstein
 Finally, Einstein's general relativity explained how gravity acts at a distance by showing that mass curves the 3 dimensions of space and the single dimension of time. This curvature, in turn, tells mass how to move, i.e., following the straightest possible path (a geodesic) through that curved space-time. Thus, it was a remarkably elegant theory that relied solely on geometry to explain how gravity can act instantaneously at any distance, in the absence of a medium. Its predictions have held to this date. The Einstein equation of curvature of space-time has a coupling constant linking geometry to matter: $8*pi*G/c^4$, 
 where

* $G$ is the gravitational constant 
* $c$ is the speed of light.

The factor of $c^4$ is huge, suggesting that space-time is incredibly stiff. Thus, a massive amount of energy or mass is needed to warp it. This explains why gravity primarily dominates on the macroscopic scale typical of astronomical phenomena.

### Pseudo-Riemannian manifold
Einstein's equations were developed in what is called a 4 dimensional Pseudo-Riemannian manifold. 

It is called Pseudo-Riemannian because in standard Riemannian geometry of 3 space dimentions we have the distance metric as:

$ds^2 = dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2$,

a generalization of the bhujA-koTi-karNa-nyAya.

However, when time is used as one of the 4 dimensions, as per special relativity, one has to account for it being the universal speed limit. Thus, if you have a flash of light, it will take the equation

$x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = (ct)^2$, 

where c is the speed of light and t is the time it has travelled. Since the speed of light is the universal speed limit for every observer $-(ct)^2 + x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 0$.

Thus, we get the Pseudo-Riemannian or Minkowski metric of 3 space and 1 time dimensions:
$ds^2 = -c^2dt^2 + dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2$

## Kaluza 5D
About 110 years ago, a German polymath, Theodor Kaluza (who knew 17 languages), asked the question of what might happen if he used a 5-dimensional Pseudo-Riemannian manifold, with one extra space dimension, instead of Einstein's 4-dimensional one:

$dS^2 = -(ct)^2 + dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2 + ds^2$

The result was remarkable: the resulting equivalents of Einstein’s field equations in five dimensions  naturally split into two parts:

* The original 4D Einstein equations (Gravity).
* A set of equations that are identical to Maxwell’s equations, yielding electromagnetism purely from geometry.

This suggested that the forces could all potentially fall out of geometry alone.+++(5)+++ 

## Klein
About 100 years ago, the Swedish mathematical physicist Oskar Klein arrived at the same 5-dimensional formulation as Kaluza, but with some improvements, including the incorporation of quantum mechanics. As a result, he explained why we do not see the 5th dimension – it is too minute and curled up as a circle of the order of Planck's length ($1.616 \times 10^{-35} \text{ meters}$). 

Moreover, as per this theory, the conservation of momentum, which we see in 4 dimensions, is the regular eponymous physical law. 

However, the same law in the 5th dimension, which is quantized, manifests as the law of conservation of charge in our ordinarily 4D world. These rather remarkable correspondences suggested that the Kaluza-Klein theory was capturing something real about the universe. 

## Drawbacks
However, it was an incomplete theory because it did not account for the two other forces, the weak force and the strong force. Moreover, it has a third component beyond electromagnetism, the scalar "Dilaton field," for which there is no physical evidence in the form seen in this theory.

### String theory
However, the Kaluza-Klein theory has been the founding inspiration for the line of investigation leading to modern string theories, with its multiple extra dimensions. It was the first theory that showed that our 4D world is likely just a "slice" of a more multidimensional reality. While the modern successor theories are vastly more mathematically sophisticated, even after a whole century of investigation, we do not know whether any of these successor theories are descriptions of the real-world physics.
