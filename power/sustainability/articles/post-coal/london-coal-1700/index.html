Restorations: Coal, Smoke, and Time in London, circa 1700 Aleksandr Bierig

Early modern London was the planet‚Äôs first coal-fired city.+++(5)+++ While the causes and timing of its transition to fossil fuel are still debated,
historians have argued that the change was initially triggered in the early seventeenth century,
when apparent timber shortages began driving up firewood prices in the metropolis.
At the same time, the chance existence of waterways near the coalfields of Durham and Northumberland, three hundred miles north of London,+++(5)+++
afforded the possibility of shipping fossil fuel along the eastern English seaboard during a period of highly constrained transportation.
While coal had been extracted from these areas for centuries,
its scale was always limited;
coal remained a specialty fuel,
considered appropriate only for heat-intensive activities like firing limekilns or blacksmithing.

But in the decades around 1600,
necessity and circumstance compelled Londoners to dramatically expand their consumption.
The city adopted coal‚Äîpreviously considered too dirty and noxious for widespread use‚Äîas its general fuel.1

As London embarked on its experiment in fossil fuel use,
how would coal-fired cities become different from previous ones?
First and foremost, fossil fuel provided London with a novel and powerful source of heat.
On the whole, summarizes Derek Keene,
coal ‚Äúendowed London with a virtually unlimited source of energy to support its physical growth.‚Äù2
To take one striking example, coal was crucial to the reconstruction of London in brick following the Great Fire of 1666;
in the aftermath of the destruction,
as many as 100 million bricks were manufactured each year,
a scale and speed of production that would have been impossible with any other fuel.3+++(5)+++
From a broader perspective, Britain‚Äôs adoption of fossil fuel allowed for an unfamiliar, indeed unprecedented, type of urban, economic, and demographic growth.4

Beyond these sweeping changes, which unfolded over decades and generations,
the most infamous and immediately perceptible consequence of London‚Äôs early coal use was smoke.
Above all, urban coal smoke was sensible to sight, smell, and taste.
In one of the earliest evocations of London‚Äôs pollution‚Äî
Fumifugium, or, The inconveniencie of the aer and smoak of London dissipated together with some remedies humbly proposed (1661)‚Äî
botanist and diarist John Evelyn expressed his ‚ÄúIndignation‚Äù that London, ‚Äúthis Glorious and Antient City,‚Äù should suffer a condition that ‚Äúdarkens and eclipses all her other Attributes.‚Äù5+++(5)+++
In his pamphlet, Evelyn seemed particularly concerned with the effects of pollution on human health,
anticipating another theme that would persist for centuries.
He claimed that Londoners were subject to the ‚Äúdisordering [of] the entire habits of their Bodies;
so that Catharrs, Phthisticks, Coughs and Consumptions rage more in this one City than the whole Earth besides.‚Äù6
Coal smoke, he noted, also seemed to affect other living things.
When coal supplies temporarily ceased during the English Civil War a decade earlier,
Evelyn recalled that ‚ÄúGardens and Orchards planted even in the very heart of London,
were observed to bear such plentiful and infinite quantities of Fruits,
as they never produced the like either before or since.‚Äù7

London‚Äôs smoke, like its use of coal more generally,
was aberrant, and it produced aberrations. The city‚Äôs early entanglement with fossil fuel had enabled explosive growth,
but it also seemed to disorder life in the city.

These themes are well known. Many scholars have explored how urban pollution became the sign of London‚Äôs anomalous source of energy, offering visible evidence of the unplanned consequences introduced by the widespread consumption of fossil fuel.
William Cavert, who recently published the definitive history of pollution in early modern London, has shown how, as early as the seventeenth century,
‚Äúsmoky air became a symbol of urban life.‚Äù8
His comprehensive scholarship complements scores of studies on urban pollution in London,
which have tended to focus on its regulation, its effects on health, and its presence in literary culture.9
These writers cast atmospheric pollution as a hallmark of what Cavert calls the city‚Äôs ‚Äúearly modernity,‚Äù prefiguring a more general condition of urban life in the modern period.10

The repeated use of the word ‚Äúearly‚Äù here should remind us that,
before other places adopted coal in the nineteenth century,
London‚Äôs pollution was unlike anything else in the known world.
It was also the result of circumstance.
The city lies in the Thames River valley,
providing the topographical setting for a phenomenon known as ‚Äútemperature inversion.‚Äù+++(5)+++
As cold air becomes trapped beneath warm air,
it creates a lingering fog;
in London, this atmospheric mist combined with the sulfur and particulate matter that was released from burning bituminous coal.
In addition to visible pollution, this mixture creates the compound sulfur dioxide, an acid that corrodes surfaces of all kinds.+++(5)+++
Retrospective calculations by Peter Brimblecombe and Carlota Grossi estimate that the concentration of pollutants in London‚Äôs air exceeded modern levels
as early as the mid-seventeenth century.11

If many worried about London‚Äôs pollution‚Äîwriters, natural philosophers, physicians, and politicians, as well as the scientists and scholars that followed in their wake‚Äî
very few imagined that anything could be done about such a pervasive and entrenched problem.
The two authors discussed in this essay were the exception and among the earliest to do so.
Both John Evelyn and his lesser-known contemporary Timothy Nourse began from the problem of London‚Äôs smoke,
only to find themselves pulled into acts of urban planning.
They both devised schemes to repair the city‚Äôs pollution
and, in doing so, expressed a kind of early environmental nostalgia.
Writing towards the end of the seventeenth century, Evelyn and Nourse both dreamed they might restore the city and its air to a time before London had embarked on its fossil-fueled urban life.

Their proposals, however, were very different.
Evelyn‚Äôs well-known and well-studied pamphlet, Fumifugium, put forward a spatial solution:
he proposed reorganizing London by relegating smoky industries outside of the city, beyond the poorer eastern edge of the metropolis.
By contrast, Nourse‚Äôs proposal, ‚ÄúAn Essay upon the Fuel of London‚Äù (1700), outlined a material solution, a scheme to restore wood as London‚Äôs primary fuel.
If Evelyn‚Äôs text is well known as a formative historical example of environmental and urban planning,
in this essay I will argue that it was Nourse‚Äôs plan that showed more clearly how the coal-fired city presented new problems,
which were less about the appearance of space than about the composition of a new, fossil-fueled urban metabolism that had social, spatial, and temporal consequences.12
Nourse implied that it was only by attending to this material directly‚Äîby restoring wood as the fuel of the metropolis‚Äîthat London might be returned to a more manageable state.13

A focus on Nourse‚Äôs unusual plan, in turn, offers a different view of the implications of coal smoke:
of what pollution ultimately meant for the direction and fate of the city.
Opposed to the predominating concerns with the aesthetic and medical consequences of smoke, Nourse‚Äôs text dwelled on a stranger and less immediately sensible problem:
he drew attention to the ways in which coal‚Äôs pollution seemed to speed up the material decay of the city,
dissolving the surfaces and connective tissues of its built environment‚Äîsmoke corroded London‚Äôs stone facades
and ate away at mortar joints and iron hinges.
Indeed, smoke seemed to attack any object of human fabrication, damaging buildings, furniture, tapestries, and clothing.+++(5)+++
If the visibly polluted air of London and its effects on human health have been the subject of much historical interest,
this kind of progressive material disintegration was more difficult to capture.
It did not merely affect the eyes and lungs,
but it disrupted the reliability of time itself.

In exploring these ideas,
Nourse‚Äôs essay suggested that coal had introduced a new kind of inorganic, artificial ecology that would prove fateful for this city and, eventually, all others.14
Before the modern steam engine, before coal-fired iron production, before canals and railways and fossil capital,
his attention to the interaction between coal, smoke, and the built environment suggested that
there might be an intrinsic relationship between fossil fuel and the unruly temporal relations that it unleashed.
Even at this first, urban scale, the unfamiliar cycles of production, consumption, and destruction set off by coal use had,
in a sense, accelerated the speed of time.+++(5)+++

Designs on the City: Spatial and Material

Before exploring Nourse‚Äôs essay, however, it is worth reviewing John Evelyn‚Äôs earlier text on coal, smoke, and the shape of the city. Evelyn‚Äôs Fumifugium is rightly seen as a seminal text of urban planning and, like most acts of planning, it emerged from a close connection with political power.
The twenty-six-page essay appeared in 1661, the first year of Charles II‚Äôs restoration to the English throne,
and it was directly addressed to the reinstated king.15
In offering a response to London‚Äôs pollution,
the royalist Evelyn imagined that political control over the city would be restored, just as the monarchy had been the year before. Fumifugium, according to this reading, was not so much an urban design as an articulation of an elaborate political allegory.
Evelyn‚Äôs smoke, argues historian Mark Jenner, served as ‚Äúa metaphor of the political disorder of the Interregnum and his proposal was intended as a ‚Äúpanegyric to the new regime.‚Äù16
Reinstating a smokeless urban order would give credit to the king‚Äôs restored position.

If Evelyn interpreted London‚Äôs pollution as a political problem,
he also understood it as a distinctly spatial one.
Fumifugium began by recounting how a wave of ‚Äúpresumptuous Smoake‚Äù had infiltrated the king‚Äôs palace at Whitehall,
flooding in until ‚Äúall the Rooms, Galleries, and Places about it were fill‚Äôd and infested.‚Äù17
It was this violation of physical space,
directly imposing on the body of the monarch,
that set the stage for his essay.
Evelyn cast London‚Äôs smoke as a challenge to royal authority and,
in response, he proposed that the king should eliminate pollution throughout the city as proof of his power.

Like his motivating concern, Evelyn‚Äôs solution was also spatial:
he proposed moving what he called the ‚Äúsmokey trades‚Äù beyond the southeastern edge of the city, as can be seen in a diagrammatic map (Fig. 1).
London‚Äôs ‚ÄúBrewers, Diers, Lime-burners, Salt, and Sope-boylers,‚Äù he explained, were located along the Thames River close to the city center.
One of their ‚ÄúSpiracles alone,‚Äù Evelyn judged, ‚Äúdoes manifestly infect the Aer, more than all the Chiminies of London put together besides.‚Äù18
In fact, he was probably wrong to single out these sources as the primary cause of the city‚Äôs pollution;
despite the spectacular emissions generated by workshops and breweries,
it is likely that domestic fireplaces accounted for the vast majority of emissions.19+++(5)+++
Evelyn‚Äôs reasoning, however, underscored the spatial and aesthetic nature of his inquiry.
The smoke coming from intensive coal use was concentrated and visible in the city center.
According to this logic, those activities would need to be removed.

Evelyn did not go into detail regarding how such an enormous industrial transfer might be implemented or who it would affect.
He briefly imagined that his plan would create more work for those ‚ÄúThousands of able Watermen‚Äù working the small rivercraft on the Thames‚Äîif manufacturing moved eastward,
the workers moving goods along the river would find even more employment, he wrote, bringing ‚ÄúCommodities into the City.‚Äù20
But the broader implications of this immense spatial reorganization were left unexplained.
Evelyn‚Äôs aim was to sever the visible co-dependence of the city and its dirty fuel,
not to explain the interactions and complications engendered by coal use.
Like so many later acts of urban planning and industrial zoning,
his proposal imagined a spatial segregation of production and consumption.

As a counterpart to this displacement,
Evelyn also proposed surrounding the metropolis with newly planted fields of ‚Äúthe most fragrant and odoriferous Flowers ‚Ä¶ aptest to tinge the Aer upon every gentle emission at a great distance.‚Äù21
It was not enough, in other words, simply to remove pollution from central London;
Evelyn also wanted to renovate the air itself.
In his reformed city, appearance would be paramount,
and his design aimed at the top of the social hierarchy, at the royal court and its elite audiences to whom he addressed his essay.
Had the reforms been enacted, the jumble of wharfs and factories that littered London‚Äôs riverbanks would have been replaced by grand residences for the city‚Äôs elite‚Äîa plan, Cavert summarizes, ‚Äúquite literally, to gentrify London.‚Äù22

These ideas resonated with Evelyn‚Äôs enduring belief in the political importance of architecture and urbanism.
He admired the aesthetic absolutism of Louis XIV‚Äôs France,
which he had witnessed firsthand during the English Civil War.
One of his earlier essays, A Character of England (1659),
offered ideas and plans for a redesigned London, beginning from another diagnosis of the city‚Äôs ills.23
In that essay, a fictional French visitor complained that in ramshackle London,
‚Äúthe buildings are as deformed as the minds and confusions of the people.‚Äù24

Likewise, following the Great Fire of 1666,
he offered a plan to rectify the chaos of the central city,
straightening its streets and clearing out its warrens of alleys and byways (Fig. 2).
Everywhere he looked, Evelyn seemed to find a world that needed to be brought into order.

Nourse

Some thirty years later,
London‚Äôs smoke once again prompted a writer to imagine an immense reform.
Much had changed during the intervening decades‚Äîa devastating fire and a deposed monarch‚Äîbut the city‚Äôs pollution remained persistent.
In response, Timothy Nourse‚Äôs ‚ÄúAn Essay Upon the Fuel of London‚Äù imagined planting an immense forest to surround the metropolis,
a new supply source that aimed to reinstate wood as London‚Äôs fuel.
Instead of reorganizing the space of the city,
as Evelyn had proposed, Nourse argued that its pervasive smoke could only be drawn back through a deliberate material restoration, a return to wood.
While Evelyn had briefly contemplated this same idea, he quickly dismissed it:

‚Äúto talk of serving this vast City (though Paris as great, be so supplied) with Wood [would be] madnesse.‚Äù25

As we will see, Nourse took this ‚Äúmadnesse‚Äù seriously,
methodically working through the requirements of his plan.
If London‚Äôs material relations had been disordered by the importation of coal from hundreds of miles away,
Nourse imagined that his proposed forest around London would restore a legible order.
Once wood was restored,
the long-distance supply chain that provided fossil fuel to the city would be replaced by a direct relation of interior and exterior, city and hinterland, consumers and producers.
At the same time, Nourse‚Äôs plan also underscored the immense differences that already existed between coal and wood.
In comparing the two fuels, he revealed how difficult, if not impossible,
it would prove to find an adequate substitute for the surplus energy of fossil fuel.+++(4)+++

What led Nourse to this unusual idea?
Compared to the well-known Evelyn, Nourse‚Äôs motivations are less clear.
Little is known about him.
He was, according to one account, from an ‚Äúold Royalist family which had formerly held estates in Bucks, Oxford and Hertford.‚Äù26
He studied at Oxford, became a preacher,
and wrote about religious topics.
His ‚ÄúAn Essay upon the Fuel of London,‚Äù however, appeared at the end of a long treatise on agricultural improvement,
which was initially published in 1700, the year following his death.27
Despite appearances, however, it is precisely this unusual context that helps explain aspects of Nourse‚Äôs perspective
and raises a number of troubling questions about planning and conservation in its wake.

Understanding Nourse‚Äôs proposal to return from coal to wood, in other words,
requires a detour into the content and contexts of the book it appeared in:
Campania F≈ìlix, or, A Discourse of the Benefits and Improvements of Husbandry (1700).
Like many other contemporaneous works on agricultural management,
it was a profoundly conservative text,
offering fifteen chapters of advice on how to manage a countryside estate.
Nourse offered a catalogue of techniques to both improve agricultural yields and, perhaps more importantly, to conserve the social hierarchy of the rural world.
In his view, the country landowner, the tenant farmers who paid rent,
and the poor landless cottagers each had their established place in the social order.28
This hierarchy, however, was not stable but fragile;
it depended on incentives and designs imposed from above.
‚ÄúAn Essay upon the Fuel of London‚Äù can only be understood in relation to these larger themes‚Äî
in particular, Nourse‚Äôs abiding interest in the relationship between human intentions,
material design, and the social consequences that such designs gave rise to over the course of time.

Campania F≈ìlix was a late entry in a long tradition of sixteenth- and seventeenth-century texts on agricultural ‚Äúimprovement,‚Äù
which advised landowners how best to supervise their rural holdings.
As in many of those earlier works‚Äîsuch as Walter Blith‚Äôs defining pair, The English improver (1649) and The English improver improved (1652)‚ÄîNourse‚Äôs recommendations were founded on a belief that to improve one‚Äôs estate was to carry out God‚Äôs work.29+++(5)+++
As he explained in his preface:

‚ÄúThe same spot of Ground, which some Time since was nothing but Heath and Desart,
and under the Original Curse of Thorns and Bryers after a little Labour and Expence, seems restor‚Äôd to its Primitive Beauty in the State of Paradise.‚Äù30

For Nourse, as for his improvement-minded predecessors and contemporaries, agricultural management was understood as a path towards the re-creation of a prelapsarian abundance.

Beyond these grand beliefs, however, most of Campania F≈ìlix detailed smaller problems.
It offered pages of advice on topics like the rotation of seasonal crops or how to best synchronize the pollarding of trees.
Most notably, Nourse implored landlords to think through the intricate, cascading social effects of what we might now call their design decisions.31
Take his fourth chapter, ‚ÄúOn Fences.‚Äù When building fences for fields ‚Äúnear Cities,‚Äù he explained, it was better to use ‚ÄúBanks of Earth‚Äù rather than hedges.
Near urban areas, ‚Äúthe great Numbers of Poor who inhabit the Out-skirts, who upon all Occasions, and especially in cold Weather, will make Plunder of whatsoever is combustible.‚Äù32
If hedges of hazel or holly were liable to be ransacked for fuel by the urban poor, Nourse advised that landlords could plan ahead and protect their investment by building what he called ‚ÄúDead Fences‚Äù‚Äîeither ramparts made of earth and stone‚Äîor by digging ditches to mark the edge of a property.
The very substance of a constructed boundary, he implied, carried a choreography of incentives that would have to be carefully and deliberately considered.

The larger challenge Nourse described, which would reappear in his discussion of London‚Äôs coal, was how to strike a delicate balance between private property and the public good, between individual interest and social stability.
Those who owned land held a privileged position, but it was one that relied upon other people and other things. In another chapter, he suggested that landlords should build cottages for their landless laborers, depicting these dwellings as self-contained, dignified units: ‚Äúits little inclosures lying round about it ‚Ä¶ every such Cottage seems to be an Epitome of a more Voluminous Farm.‚Äù While the rights to own property were sacred, they did not exist in isolation; ‚Äúwhen a particular Man‚Äôs Concerns is so twisted with that of others,‚Äù he wrote, ‚Äúall must either sink or swim together.‚Äù33 This led to one of Nourse‚Äôs broadest interpretations, which outlined a complicated role for the rural poor within a large story of national interest. While he mistrusted commoners, he also saw the rough independence of the rural laborer as an asset and even a national advantage‚Äîin his view, the unruliness of the rural poor also made them ideal military recruits.34 For reasons small and large, then, the landowner should cautiously respect the commoner, incentivizing, but not coddling, the landless laborer. This approach would be embodied in ditches and fences and cottages. The social and material order of things were intertwined.

The most vivid example of Nourse‚Äôs method of social management through material design appeared in the first essay appended to the main text of Campania F≈ìlix: ‚ÄúAn Essay of a Country-House.‚Äù In this text, Nourse painstakingly described the architecture and planning of a countryside villa. Landscape historians have discussed this essay, placing it in the long tradition of the idealized rural residence from Varro to Palladio.35 The essay also drew on what architectural historian James Ackerman called the ‚Äúvilla ideal‚Äù‚Äîthe villa as a particular product of the metropolitan idealization of the pastoral world.36 Nourse hinted at this lineage. Unlike the rest of his book, the ‚ÄúCountry-House‚Äù was not intended for the manager of an agricultural estate. Instead, he imagined the design for an absentee urban landowner, fit ‚Äúto lodge a Nobleman endu‚Äôd with ample Fortunes and a vertuous Mind,‚Äù a place to retreat from the city in ‚Äúthe solid and serene Enjoyments of the Country.‚Äù37

Nourse offered no drawings of this structure, but he did describe the design in thousands of words of exhaustive detail. The width of the main staircase would extend eight feet, with ‚Äúthe Rails of the Stairs [to be] laid in Oil to prevent Rust.‚Äù The windows on the upper floor should number fourteen on the front fa√ßade and seven on the lateral side. Three separate gardens would flank the house and extend into the landscape. He even suggested that ‚Äúa little Town or Village‚Äù should be designed at the perimeter of the estate, a model community filled with ‚Äúall sorts [of] Artificers and Labourers, which any Nobleman‚Äôs House can stand in need of‚Äù.38 This anticipated the form known, many decades later, as a ferme orn√©e, or ornamental farm imagined as a kind of stage set for its wealthy owner.39

At first glance, this elaborate description seemed unburdened by the complex rural social structures explored through the rest of Campania F≈ìlix. The ‚ÄúCountry-House‚Äù would cast a willful, dominating design over the landscape. In the last few pages of his essay, however, Nourse introduced an alternative to this vision that seemed at odds with what he had taken thousands of words to construct. After pages and pages of intense description, Nourse suddenly compared the plan to King Solomon‚Äôs ‚Äústately Palaces‚Äù in Ecclesiastes. Grand projects like the ‚ÄúCountry-House‚Äù might at first appear ‚Äúdivinely inspir‚Äôd,‚Äù but ‚Äúall such Delights are Vanity, as are all other contrivances and enjoyments whatsoever compar‚Äôd with what is truly durable and solid.‚Äù40 Those who could not afford an extravagant villa, he wrote, might instead build a ‚Äúlittle, well design‚Äôd House, neatly kept,‚Äù a structure that would embody the small comforts of ‚Äúthat middle Region of Happiness which lies above Oppression and Necessity, and below the Menaces and Storms to which higher Fortunes are expos‚Äôd.‚Äù41 Rather than monumentalizing power, the landowner‚Äôs house could promote a virtuous, unassuming modesty. Architecture, in these terms, might mediate the social tensions on the agricultural estate. The landowner‚Äôs house could become another instrument to maintain the fragile order of the rural world.

Coal‚Äôs Urban Ecology

Nourse‚Äôs interest in the social implications of spatial and material design reappeared in the concluding text of his book,‚ÄúAn Essay upon the Fuel of London.‚Äù Here, he positioned himself between city and country, and perhaps it was this outside perspective that allowed him to envision the replacement of the city‚Äôs fuel. He aimed to turn the teeming, polluted metropolis into a place of deliberate natural and social management. He saw the city as if it were an estate.

As in his ‚ÄúCountry-House‚Äù essay, Nourse began his closing text with a grand testimonial, celebrating London‚Äôs wide streets, its central river ‚Äúcover‚Äôd always with such Ships laden with inestimable Riches,‚Äù and its many monuments of such ‚ÄúGreatness and Solidity, as will not easily be out-fac‚Äôd by Time.‚Äù Then, once again, he reversed course. Of all the cities in Europe, he continued, ‚Äúthere is not a more nasty and unpleasant Place.‚Äù He mentioned a litany of ‚ÄúDisorders‚Äù‚Äîexcessive liquor, expensive food, and ‚ÄúIrreputable Traders‚Äù ‚Äîbut arrived at one problem that, he wrote, ‚Äúif redress‚Äôd would extreamly Contribute to the Benefit and Beauty of this City; and if continu‚Äôd, will leave it expos‚Äôd to many fatal Inconveniences.‚Äù The potentially fatal issue was, naturally, the burning of coal and its attendant smoke. ‚ÄúThis indeed is that one great Nuisance which sullies all the Beauties of the City,‚Äù Nourse wrote, noting that coal‚Äôs pollution ‚Äúmay be seen, felt, smelt, and tasted at some Miles distance, so obvious is it to all our Senses.‚Äù42

Though he began with the sensible and perceptible effects of coal smoke, Nourse seemed less concerned with human bodies than with inanimate things‚Äîhow the very material of the city was undermined by pollution. This revolved around the corrosive compound produced by burning coal‚Äîlater named sulfur dioxide‚Äîwhich insinuated itself into the materials of the metropolis, disintegrating them with an astonishing speed. Evelyn had also described this problem when he wrote that the city‚Äôs pollution left

‚Äúa sooty Crust or furr upon all that it lights, spoyling the moveables, tarnishing the Plate Gildings and Furniture, and corroding the very Iron-bars and hardest stones with those piercing and acrimonious Spirits which accompany its Sulphure.‚Äù

Physical things subjected to this atmosphere, Evelyn estimated, decayed ‚Äúmore in one year, then expos‚Äôd to the pure Aer of the Country it could effect in some hundreds.‚Äù43

London‚Äôs coal smoke seemed to accelerate time‚Äôs work, a temporal disruption that became particularly evident on the buildings of the metropolis. Nourse‚Äôs detailed description of this phenomenon is worth quoting in full:

And such truly is the Corroding Quality of this Smoak, that the hardest Things in Nature, or made by Art, cannot resist it; witness the Bars and Casements of Windows, the Balconeys, with sorts of Iron-work, which though never so well Oil‚Äôd and Polish‚Äôd, will in a few Years become Eaten and Mouldring with Rust, and must after a short Time be renew‚Äôd to become fresh Fuel for this all-devouring Smoak. The Stones themselves run the same Fate ‚Ä¶ eaten away peel‚Äôd and fley‚Äôd as I may say to the very Bones by this hellish and subterraneous Flume. ‚Ä¶ ‚ÄôTwere endless to reckon up all the Mischiefs which Houses suffer hereby in their Furniture, their Plate, their Brass and Pewter, their Glass, with whatsoever is solid and refin‚Äôd, all which are Corroded by it. ‚Ä¶ Nay so piercing is this smoak, that it works itself betwixt the joints of Bricks, and eats out the Mortar; so that what was Fresh and Beautiful Twenty or Thirty years ago, now looks Black, Old and Decay‚Äôd [‚Ä¶]44

If London‚Äôs visibly polluted air has long served as the most noted register of the city‚Äôs precocious coal-dependence, this smoke perhaps obscured another, more elusive index: the accelerated wearing-away of stone, mortar, iron, and fabric. As pollution compromised the presumed solidity of architecture and other objects of human manufacture, the larger implications of coal use became evident. Fossil fuel had upended the physical stability of the city, disrupting the reliable and familiar rhythms of a wood-based energy system. Its smoke attacked not just the durability of materials, but also challenged the underlying assumption that things would stay the same‚Äîthe ideological connection, as Elaine Tierney has suggested, between ‚Äúpermanence and progress.‚Äù45 Coal undermined the expectation that tomorrow would, in some sense, look like yesterday.

Where Evelyn diagnosed a visually and spatially disordered world, Nourse recognized how coal use had given rise to a set of recursive, spiraling relations that unfolded over time‚Äîwhat we would now refer to as feedback loops.46

For instance, he identified a recursive relation between visible pollution and the need to burn fuel. London‚Äôs coal smoke was particularly bad in winter months, meaning that ‚Äúthe force of the Winter-Sun is not able to scatter‚Äù and the city‚Äôs inhabitants were consistently cast into darkness, ‚Äútotally depriv‚Äôd of the warmth and comforts of the Day.‚Äù+++(5)+++ In response, Londoners were compelled ‚Äúto make more Fires than ordinary, so that the more Fire the more Smoak; and the more Smoak the more need of Fire.‚Äù47+++(5)+++ Nourse also noted that the material disintegration caused by pollution had necessitated a different and more constant kind of physical maintenance of the city‚Äôs architectural fabric. Coal smoke‚Äôs rapid decay meant that ‚Äúonce in an Age or Two there must be Rebuilding, or continual Repairing in a manner the whole City.‚Äù48 Though he admitted that coal was a more concentrated fuel‚Äî‚Äúone Chaldron of Coal will yield more heat than Three or Four Loads of Wood‚Äù‚Äîhe questioned whether the costs of this dense power were too extensive.49
If coal provided an easy and ready heat, ‚Äúso it is as true too,‚Äù Nourse wrote, ‚Äúthat the Dammage sustain‚Äôd by a House in London, or any Figure or Trade, by the smutty smoak of the Coal, is triple to the extraordinary Charge such a House would ly under, were it obliged only to make use of Wood and Char-coal.‚Äù50

If these consequences affected every house and trade, they also left their mark on the city‚Äôs central monuments‚Äîmost notably, St. Paul‚Äôs Cathedral, then being reconstructed by Christopher Wren (a project paid for, in part, by a tax on coal imports into London).51
He warned that the cathedral ‚Äúso Stately and Beautiful as it is, will after an Age or Two, look old and discolour‚Äôd before ‚Äôtis finish‚Äôd.‚Äù Its intricate details, Nourse continued, had only provided more surface area to become ‚Äúfurr‚Äôd and sooty by the Smoak sticking to it, and in a short time be defac‚Äôd.‚Äù52
In this atmosphere of decay, he concluded, it would be ‚Äúimpossible for any Man to live sweet and clean, to appear polite and well-adjusted amidst so many inevitable inconveniencies, without a vast Expence.‚Äù53
Smoke seemed to attack all things ‚Äúsolid and refin‚Äôd‚Äù; the construction of a stable material world was difficult to conserve in the face of coal‚Äôs destructive effects.

The deeper irony was that coal‚Äôs energy had allowed for the fabrication of more solid things than ever before.

It fueled a rapid increase in the production of materials that had granted a new physical permanence to the city‚Äîits bricks, mortar, glass and, later, iron.
What Nourse revealed was that smoke ate away at the very things that coal had helped produce.
Held within the frame of this first coal-fired city, fossil fuel had unleashed dense and intensive feedbacks of creation and destruction.
The solid objects afforded by coal‚Äôs energy could not withstand its corrosive air.
Though this relationship was not a straightforward, reciprocal one‚Äîan equal and opposite reaction‚Äîthere was some sense in which the things that coal had given to London, its smoke took away.54

Architectural historians writing about later developments have commented on the material decay caused by modern pollution.
In different studies, Timothy Hyde, Edward Gillin, and Jorge Otero-Pailos have each shown how the problem of pollution informed and challenged notions of legal, scientific, and aesthetic judgment in the nineteenth century, inciting ideas to reform the selection of building materials, architectural design, and aesthetic judgment to deal with the accelerated decay of coal smoke.55
In those later episodes, however, pollution had become a necessary consequence of fossil-fueled modernity, whereas for Nourse, coal smoke remained a specific, local phenomenon.
Since pollution and its effects were, circa 1700, more unusual, perhaps they were easier to recognize in their full implications.+++(5)+++
Perhaps this is why, in contrast to the reactive measures offered later‚Äîto regulate emissions, for instance, or to find a stone or chemical finish that might withstand the damaging effects of pollution‚ÄîNourse could still imagine confronting the problem at its root.

Keeping Time

Nourse‚Äôs diagnosis of coal‚Äôs cascading consequences‚Äîthe unfamiliar, artificial ecology that fossil fuel had already generated by the end of the seventeenth century‚Äîled to his radical proposal to replace coal with wood. Where Evelyn imagined fields of flowers as urban ornaments, Nourse presented a distributed plan that encompassed natural and social effects. Where Evelyn‚Äôs spatial scheme stood as a reflection of royal authority, political circumstances had changed. Nourse suggested his planned forest would only be possible through an Act of Parliament, since ‚Äúwithout the All-mightiness of a Parliament no Great and Public Work can ever come to any Maturity.‚Äù56

He worked through the material requirements of his idea. ‚ÄúTo understand what Quantity of Fuel may be sufficient for this great City,‚Äù Nourse wrote, ‚Äúwe must enquire into its Bigness, the Number of its Inhabitants, and the Circumstances of the Climate.‚Äù57 How many people lived in London, and how much coal did they actually burn? How could its dense power be replaced by the less potent heat provided by firewood?
Nourse sketched out rough guesses, approximating the population of London, the number of chimneys in the city, and, from that estimate, the extent of the forest that would be needed to grow enough wood to meet demand.
Historians discussing Nourse‚Äôs proposal have pointed out that these calculations placed him within an emergent trend of large-scale quantification.
Nourse wrote around the same time that early practitioners of political arithmetic like William Petty and John Graunt attempted to capture city and nation in quantitative form. These calculative efforts also followed others who had tried to quantify estimated yields, tallying the expected output of managed forests and fields.58

Precise statistical estimation, however, was not the point of Nourse‚Äôs essay.
His estimates were informed guesses and probably not particularly accurate.
More striking was the way in which,
just like his analysis of the unfolding consequences of coal pollution, he followed his reasoning both forward and backwards in a concatenation of accounts,
each one informing the next in a chain.
He did not simply attempt to estimate statistical totals, but constantly translated between them:
London‚Äôs population was approximated in order to extrapolate aggregate fuel use;
this number, in turn, informed how much woodland would be needed to replace coal‚Äôs heat,
which itself depended on expected forest yields.
Rather than offering a detailed quantitative assessment,
he sketched the complex artificial ecology that coal had already generated.
Wishing to draw it backwards,
he then outlined his alternative vision of a return to wood
and tried to imagine what social and natural relations this restoration would bring about.

In the remainder of ‚ÄúAn Essay upon the Fuel of London,‚Äù Nourse tried to fill in the gaps that his design seemed to leave open, to show that a well-managed wood supply could provide the same power as coal, without its increasingly evident environmental costs. As with his guidelines for estate management, his scheme to plant thousands of acres of woodland simultaneously affected both the natural and social worlds.
Scores of laborers would have to tend to the woodlands, maintain its pollarding, and manage the transportation of firewood into the city. Once again, he saw an opportunity for social and natural management. So much work would result from the continuous maintenance of these forests, he argued, that every hundred acres would ‚Äúvery well Employ and Maintain Four Families for ever.‚Äù Through this employment program, he concluded, ‚Äúthe poorer sort of People will be double gainers by the Bargain.‚Äù61 Nourse had imagined a vast plantation filled with (if we follow his estimate) 2,400 families dotted through the forest, tending to its continuous cultivation. Worried that the woodland might become a target for ‚ÄúThieves, and Robbers with which such Places are but too much infested,‚Äù he also suggested that every thousand acres should be assigned a local bailiff, ‚Äúwhose business it should be continually to visit the Woods under his care, and to give an Account of what may occur to his notice.‚Äù62 The plan offered a re-oriented natural hinterland and a welfare scheme all at once. It was a conservative wooden utopia, which would be monitored by the state and filled with docile laborers.

Replacing the fuel of London also meant the interruption of countless coal-fired systems that had already taken flight, and Nourse considered several of these in his final pages. What would happen to the northern coal owners and the miners they employed? He dismissed this problem with an appeal to the public good: coal sickened the miners and polluted the metropolis, providing profit for only a few.63
He also conceded that coal was less ‚Äúcumbersome‚Äù than wood‚Äîit was a more concentrated fuel that took up less space‚Äî
and so he envisioned a network of ovens distributed throughout his forest,
where the wooden harvest would be refined into a denser, purer charcoal.64
The advantages of returning to wood, Nourse argued, would bring their own positive changes.
It would reverse recent tendencies in land use that had incentivized landowners to ‚Äúconvert their barren Grounds into Wood-Plantations‚Äù
instead of yielding to the temptation to ‚Äúquit the Preservation‚Äù of forested land because of the availability of cheap coal.65
Finally, Nourse addressed the tax on coal imports that was then funding the rebuilding of St. Paul‚Äôs Cathedral. Implementing his ‚ÄúProject of Wood-fires,‚Äù he wrote, would take a long time; at least twenty-five years, according to his estimates. Through that delay, the coal tax could continue and St. Paul‚Äôs would be finished: ‚Äúthat admirable Fabrick may arrive to its Consummation.‚Äù66

Here again was Nourse‚Äôs attention to cascading consequences. Human decisions depended on material substrates, which then informed further questions that unfolded over time. Fuel was foundational in this regard. He showed how a coal-fired city precipitated a set of social, spatial, and material relations, while a wood-fueled metropolis would require different ones. If coal had initiated progressively faster cycles between fossil fuel use and its surrounding environment, a return to wood promised to reinstate slower and more manageable speeds. Nourse was, in effect, proposing a scheme of what we would now call de-carbonization, but his conservative utopia reveals what such schemes of material deceleration really involve. It is difficult to get rid of smoke without getting rid of fire, and getting rid of fire threatens to slow everything down. A city‚Äôs fuel was not just another commodity or raw material, but it served as a regulator for the whole social-natural system.

What finally emerged from these spiraling interactions between London, its fuel, and its social, material, and spatial management was something very different. Nourse revealed coal‚Äôs challenge to posterity. The preeminent English-language historian of early modern European wood use, Paul Warde, has argued that the problems raised by woodland management led to the consideration of unusually long horizons of time. Harvesting wood from a sapling took several years, and some tree species could take decades or even human lifetimes to reach maturity. ‚ÄúWhen you planted a tree you were planning ahead,‚Äù Warde summarizes. ‚ÄúThe consequences of decisions taken in woodland management stretched far into the future.‚Äù67 Forest management necessarily raised questions about what we would now call ‚Äúsustainability,‚Äù which in turn led to issues of governance and regulation. Planning nature towards social and political ends meant creating institutional structures that could bridge between past yields, present demands, and anticipated needs; it required considering how to span between individual citizens, households, and larger scales of natural, social, and political organization. Many writers in England, Germany, and elsewhere attended to these intertwined concerns, and Nourse inherited their tradition. In his brief and incisive discussion of ‚ÄúAn Essay on the Fuel of London,‚Äù Warde calls Nourse the ‚Äúlast of the wood projectors of the seventeenth century,‚Äù arguing that he occupied a ‚Äútransitory moment‚Äù between the long tradition of projecting agricultural output and the then-emergent methods of political arithmetic and, later, political economy, with its new attention to the problems of demography, supply, and demand.68

Nourse‚Äôs text, however, was not simply an exercise in woodland planning; instead it imagined that a designed forest might counter the revolutionary pace unleashed by London‚Äôs coal. In this way, the essay suggests how we must run thought experiments in multiple directions. If the deliberate management of farm or woodland compelled a cyclical planning that corresponded to the repetition of annual growing seasons, ‚ÄúAn Essay upon the Fuel of London‚Äù suggested, perhaps for the first time, that the time of coal would be different. It would proceed directionally, rather than cyclically; it would also proceed faster, creating and destroying with a new and unfamiliar intensity. Exchanging repeating seasons for subterranean forests of energy, fossil fuel would drive time deeper and deeper in one direction.69

Nourse concluded his text wistfully, admitting his ‚ÄúProject of wood-fires‚Äù was unlikely to be realized: ‚Äúin truth all Great and Profitable Designs whatsoever are the Issues of Time, and Things of greatest Maturity and Duration are longest in their Concession.‚Äù70 Fearing an accelerating future, Nourse imagined that his society could still gather itself to act with posterity in mind. His central contention throughout Campania F≈ìlix was that a judicious elite should be capable of designing its way out of collective problems in the disorderly city as much as on the managed estate. But as his meditation on material decay showed, fossil fuel had rendered designs on the future dangerously fragile. Coal use seemed, in a word, short-sighted. Restoring wood as the fuel of the city would recreate a metropolis in which things lasted as long as they should. Only an intentional redesign could slow or, better yet, reverse the inexorable momentum that coal had already set in motion.

In another sense, however, the project belonged to a previous era. By 1700, London had become entirely inconceivable without coal, and imagining the metropolis otherwise required the suspension of disbelief.+++(5)+++
Nourse, more than Evelyn, seemed to recognize this condition;
he showed how coal had set off a chain reaction, reforming the expectations of the entire city it now fueled.71
In time, the power, speed, and convenience of fossil fuels would prove difficult to refuse, despite their tortuous drawbacks.
But at this early moment, Nourse glimpsed a material contradiction that seemed to inhere within this unruly source of energy. Through his plan and through his attention to the life of things in the polluted city, he intimated the affronts to time‚Äôs operation that would continue to unsettle all fossil-fueled societies.
